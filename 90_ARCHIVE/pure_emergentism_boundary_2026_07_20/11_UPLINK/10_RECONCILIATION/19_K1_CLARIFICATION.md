# K1 CLARIFICATION

**Evidence tier:** [I]  
*Organism document. Interpretive operational content. Bounded by current system state.*

## Fixed issuance, continuous governance weight

This note clarifies the meaning of K1.

The phrase “100 fixed supply forever” is easy to misread.
It does not necessarily mean:
- 100 indivisible seats,
- 100 whole-number governance blocks,
- or a polity structured as fixed discrete chairs.

What it means, in the current canonical interpretation, is this:

> The total issuance is fixed at 100 base units.
> Those units are fractionally expressible to protocol precision.
> Economic rights and voting power scale proportionally with owned fraction.
> No additional units may be minted after initialization.

---

## 1. What K1 actually protects

K1 protects:
- issuance scarcity
- anti-dilution
- fixed total base
- constitutional predictability of supply

K1 does not, by itself, imply:
- indivisible governance seats
- equal-person political representation
- one-whole-token-only voting
- discrete constitutional chairs

Those are different design choices.

---

## 2. The current model

The current model is best described as:

# Fixed supply, continuous governance weight

That means:
- total supply = 100
- divisibility = allowed to token precision
- voting power = proportional to fraction held
- economic rights = proportional to fraction held
- new issuance = forbidden after initialization

So, for example:
- 1 token = 1% of total issuance
- 0.1 token = 0.1%
- 0.00000001 token = corresponding fractional governance and economic weight

This is a continuous model, not a discrete seat model.

---

## 3. Why this matters

Without this clarification, the phrase “100 fixed supply” can create a false constitutional picture.

It may sound like:
- fixed seats,
- indivisible sovereign units,
- or a senate-like political structure.

That is not what a linearly divisible token with proportional voting implies.

What it actually creates is fractional constitutional capital.

---

## 4. What K1 does NOT decide

K1 alone does not settle the deeper governance question:

> Should governance be fully proportional and continuously divisible?

That is a separate design decision.

Possible models include:

### A. Pure proportional model
- every fractional token carries proportional vote
- simplest interpretation of current token logic

### B. Hybrid model
- economic rights are fractional
- some governance rights are discretized, thresholded, or quorum-gated

### C. Seat model
- token economically divisible
- but constitutional authority maps onto distinct non-fractional seats

If the implementation currently assumes 0.00000001 token = 0.00000001 vote, then the system is using Model A.

That is acceptable if it is deliberate.
It must not be confused with seat-based constitutionalism.

---

## 5. Political implication

A fixed-supply continuously divisible governance token reduces dilution risk, but it does not by itself eliminate concentration risk.

This means anti-capture must come from more than K1 alone.

Other layers remain necessary:
- eta = 0
- K2
- Grace Exit
- refusal
- constitutional limits
- reconciliation discipline
- explicit governance rules

K1 secures issuance. It does not automatically secure political equality.

---

## 6. Canonical wording going forward

Prefer this language:

> K1 = fixed total issuance, fractionally expressible, non-inflationary governance-capital

or

> K1 fixes supply, not denomination granularity

Avoid vague phrases like:
- 100 seats forever
- 100 indivisible units
- 100 constitutional chairs

unless the governance system actually enforces that.

---

## 7. Final formulation

# K1 — Fixed issuance, continuous governance weight
The total supply is fixed at 100 base units.
Those units may be fractionally expressed to protocol precision.
Economic rights and voting power scale proportionally with held fraction.
No additional units may be minted after initialization.

---

## Closing

The invariant is:
- fixed issuance

not:
- indivisible denomination

This clarification matters because constitutional language must match implemented political reality.
