# PEER REVIEW: Paper 2 — Why 1 Is Not a Prime

## ROUTING
**Specialist required:** Number theorist or ring theorist, ideally familiar with history of prime definitions
**Source file:** `../../../07_DISSEMINATION/07_PAPERS/01_MATHEMATICAL_CORE/PAPER_02_WHY_ONE_IS_NOT_A_PRIME.md`
**Evidence tier:** [E]/[S] mixed
**Depends on:** Paper 1

---

## CORE THESIS

1 is not merely excluded from the primes by convention (for FTA uniqueness) — it is excluded by **geometric necessity**. 1 is a "transcendental" (the ground of arithmetic, the equator of S²), not a number among numbers. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic's uniqueness reflects this ontological distinction.

---

## ESTABLISHED MATHEMATICS USED

- The FTA requires 1's exclusion for unique factorization — standard
- In ring theory, 1 is a unit, not an irreducible element — standard
- Historical evolution of prime definitions (Euclid included 1, modern math excludes it) — accurate
- The unit group of Z is {±1} — standard

---

## CLAIMS REQUIRING SCRUTINY

### Claim 1: "1 is not a number"
In ZFC, 1 = {∅} is an element of ℕ. In Peano arithmetic, 1 = S(0) is a natural number. The claim that "1 is not a number but a transcendental" contradicts standard formulations. The paper must be operating in a different foundational framework — but this framework is not axiomatized.

### Claim 2: "The FTA excludes 1 by geometry, not convention"
The paper argues that the "ground is not the figure" and that 1 (the equator/ground) cannot be counted among its own outputs (the numbers/primes). This is a philosophical argument, not a number-theoretic theorem. The reviewer should assess whether a geometric necessity is demonstrated or whether this restates the well-known unit/irreducible distinction in metaphorical language.

### Claim 3: Hierarchy of mathematical objects (ground → primes → composites)
The paper proposes Level 0 (transcendentals: {0, 1, ∞}), Level 1 (primes as atoms), Level 2+ (composites). This is not a standard classification in any formal theory. In ring theory, units and irreducibles are standard categories, but the "transcendental" level is novel and undefined.

---

## WHAT A SKEPTICAL REVIEWER WOULD SAY

"The observation that 1 is a unit, not a prime, is universally known. The paper correctly describes this but then claims it reflects 'geometry' rather than 'convention.' However: (a) the FTA is a theorem in rings, not in projective geometry; (b) the distinction between ground and figure is borrowed from phenomenology, not from number theory; (c) the Riemann sphere has no natural analogue in the factorization of integers. The paper is interesting philosophy but does not constitute a number-theoretic contribution."
