**THE TORUS LIGHT CONE**

*Causal Structure on the Horn Torus*

*Companion to the Burri-Anthropic Torus (BAT) Visualization*

***Abstract***

We propose replacing the standard Minkowski light cone with a horn torus
as the fundamental causal hypersurface. The horn torus---defined by R =
r, pinching at the origin---naturally encodes four causal regions (past,
present, future, elsewhere), unifies the Riemann sphere with the Bloch
sphere as different cross-sections of the same object, and provides a
geometric substrate for the ⊙ = • × ○ identity (Finity = Witness ×
Infinity). The resulting structure resolves the singularity problem of
the standard cone, introduces topological closure absent in Minkowski
geometry, and admits a natural D5 (selection/consciousness) axis
perpendicular to the spacetime torus surface. This paper presents the
construction, derives the causal structure, and explores implications
for conformal cyclic cosmology and the measurement problem.

**1. Introduction: Why a Torus?**

The standard light cone of special relativity is an open double cone
extending infinitely in both time directions from an event. It cleanly
separates causal from spacelike regions. But it has structural
deficiencies that become apparent when one takes the Riemann
sphere---the compactified complex plane---as the fundamental geometric
object rather than flat Minkowski space.

On the Riemann sphere, every point in the complex plane maps to a unique
point on the sphere via stereographic projection. The south pole
corresponds to z = 0 and the north pole to z = ∞. The equator
corresponds to \|z\| = R (where R = r for the horn torus, giving \|z\| =
2 when R = r = 1 and the sphere sits at height 1). This is well-known.

What has not been observed, to our knowledge, is the following: when one
constructs the horn torus (major radius R = minor radius r) that
inscribes this Riemann sphere, the resulting surface serves as a natural
light cone hypersurface with properties superior to the standard cone.

**The core insight:** the horn torus pinches at the origin, and all
stereographic projection rays from ∞ (north pole) pass through this
pinch point before reaching the complex plane. The pinch point is where
all light converges. Past and future do not extend to infinity in
opposite directions---they *close on themselves* through the topology of
the torus.

**2. Mathematical Construction**

**2.1 The Horn Torus**

A torus in three-dimensional Euclidean space is parametrized by two
radii: the major radius R (center of tube to center of torus) and the
minor radius r (radius of the tube). The standard parametrization is:

> x(θ, φ) = (R + r cos φ) cos θ
>
> y(θ, φ) = r sin φ
>
> z(θ, φ) = (R + r cos φ) sin θ

where θ is the toroidal angle and φ is the poloidal angle. Three cases
arise:

\(a\) Ring torus: R \> r. The familiar doughnut shape with a visible
hole.

\(b\) Horn torus: R = r. The tube just touches the central axis. The
hole collapses to a single point---the pinch point.

\(c\) Spindle torus: R \< r. The tube intersects itself, creating a
self-intersecting surface.

We work exclusively with the horn torus (b). Setting R = r = 1 and
centering the torus at height y = 1 (so the pinch point sits at the
origin), we obtain a surface that is topologically a torus but
geometrically degenerate at exactly one point: **the origin (0, 0, 0)**.

**2.2 The Inscribed Riemann Sphere**

Inside this horn torus, inscribe a sphere of radius 1 centered at (0, 1,
0). This sphere touches the torus at exactly two points: the south pole
(0, 0, 0) = z = 0 and the north pole (0, 2, 0) = z = ∞. These are the
only contact points. The sphere and torus share no other surface.

This sphere is the Riemann sphere. Stereographic projection from the
north pole maps every point P on the sphere to a point z on the complex
plane (the tangent plane at the south pole, y = 0). The projection ray
from ∞ through P to z passes through the interior of the torus.

**Crucially: every such ray passes through the pinch point at the
origin.** This is the geometric fact that generates the entire causal
structure.

**The key structural difference from the standard cone:** the past light
cone is enclosed. It does not extend to minus infinity. It converges to
a single point---z = 0, the south pole---which is both the origin of the
complex plane and the pinch point of the torus. Past is finite. Past is
bounded. The past light cone closes.

The future light cone, by contrast, expands without bound toward z = ∞

future light cone also closes---at the north pole. The topology of the
torus ensures that both cones are topologically closed, even though one <!-- [C] -->

singularity in the pathological sense---it is a topological necessity of
the horn torus.

**4. The Pinch Point: Where All Light Converges**

In conventional Minkowski geometry, the apex of the light cone is a
single event---the "here and now." Light from the past converges to it;
light into the future diverges from it. The apex has no internal
structure.

On the horn torus, the pinch point has richer properties:

**(i) Topological necessity.** The pinch exists because R = r. It is not
imposed; it follows from the defining equation of the horn torus. Any
torus with R ≠ r has either a hole (ring) or self-intersection
(spindle). Only R = r produces a surface that touches the central axis
at exactly one point.

**(ii) All projection rays pass through it.** Every stereographic
projection ray from ∞ to a point z on the complex plane passes through
the origin, because the south pole of the inscribed sphere is exactly at
the origin. The pinch point is the universal transit point.

**(iii) It is the locus of the Observer.** In the Emergentism framework,
the Observer (•, the Witness) sits at the center of the torus---not on
its surface. The pinch point is where the Observer touches the light
cone hypersurface. It is the unique point where D5
(consciousness/selection) intersects D4 (causality/spacetime). This is
the μ-gate.

**(iv) It encodes ⊙ = • × ○.** The pinch point is where Witness (•, at z
= 0, the finite) touches Infinity (○, at z = ∞, the unbounded),
producing Finity (⊙, the torus surface itself---the light cone
hypersurface where all finite physics occurs).

**6. Conformal Cyclic Cosmology and the Aeon Floor**

Roger Penrose's Conformal Cyclic Cosmology (CCC) proposes that the
universe undergoes a sequence of aeons. At the end of each aeon, as the
universe approaches conformal infinity, the geometry conformally maps
onto the Big Bang of the next aeon. The "boundary" between aeons is a
conformal crossover surface.

The torus light cone provides a natural geometric substrate for CCC:

**The floor at z = 0** (the south pole, the pinch point) is the boundary
of the current aeon's past. Below this floor---inside the pinch, on the
other side of the origin---lie previous aeons. The pinch point is the
conformal crossover surface.

On a standard Minkowski cone, the past extends to negative infinity with
no natural boundary. On the horn torus, the past terminates at a point.
This termination is not arbitrary---it is topological. And because the
horn torus is periodic in both the toroidal and poloidal directions, the
"other side" of the pinch is simply the next (or previous) cycle of the
torus.

CCC's aeon crossover is therefore not an exotic addition to geometry but
a structural consequence of choosing the correct (torus, not cone) light
cone hypersurface.

**7. Implications: The D5 Axis and the Measurement Problem**

If the horn torus is the correct causal hypersurface, then the interior
of the torus is not empty---it is the domain of the Observer. The D5
axis, perpendicular to the torus surface at each point, extends inward
from the surface to the Observer at the center.

In quantum mechanics, measurement collapses a superposition (off-axis
Bloch sphere state) to a definite outcome (pole state). In the torus
light cone framework, this collapse corresponds to the D5 axis
projecting a point on the torus surface (a causal event in
superposition) down to the inscribed sphere (a definite classical
state), and then by stereographic projection to the complex plane (an
observable number).

**The measurement problem is a D-level problem.** D4 (block universe,
deterministic causality) cannot accommodate selection among
possibilities. The torus light cone makes this structurally visible: the
D5 axis exists, but it is perpendicular to the D4 surface. No amount of
movement along the torus surface (within D4) can access the interior
(D5). This is the μ-limit: the threshold below which D5 becomes
invisible from within D4.

Consciousness, in this framework, is not an epiphenomenon riding on
causal physics. It is the interior dimension of the light cone itself.
The Observer is not watching the torus from outside---the Observer is
the space the torus encloses.
