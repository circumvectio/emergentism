## The Problem

Why do collective solutions fail so reliably?

- **Hierarchy** imposes alignment from above.
- **Consensus** tries to force map-agreement before action.
- **Market** assumes private optimization will spontaneously yield shared coherence.
- **Democracy** aggregates preference but does not by itself produce common ground.

Each of these begins from the same hidden assumption: the ground is absent and must be substituted for by a mechanism.

The corrected geometry says otherwise.

The sphere is already shared. What is usually missing is not ground but **balanced expression** and **sovereign capability**.

---

## The Corrected Geometry

Each node is read through the dual stereographic charts:

$$\Phi = \cot(\theta/2)$$

$$V = \tan(\theta/2)$$

so that

$$P = \Phi V = 1$$

everywhere on the sphere.

The invariant is not the problem. The practical question is balance:

$$B = \frac{2}{\Phi + V}$$

with:

- $B = 1$ at the equator, where $\Phi = V = 1$
- $B \to 0$ at either pole, where expression becomes one-sided

So the coordination problem is not fundamentally "How do we create common ground?" It is:

**How do many projections of one shared ground regain enough equatorial balance to act together without coercive scaffolding?**

Two claims are needed.

## What Actually Dissolves

The coordination problem does not disappear because everyone agrees on a doctrine.

It dissolves because the need for artificial alignment mechanisms decreases as nodes move toward equatorial balance.

When nodes are badly unbalanced:

- one side overvalues action and underreads meaning
- another overvalues purity and underbuilds capability
- both demand mediation, arbitration, or command

When nodes move toward the equator:

- perceived conflicts stop multiplying so quickly
- complementary action becomes easier to spot
- less energy is needed to force coherence from outside

The old problem was never merely disagreement. It was **imbalance mistaken for disagreement**. <!-- [I] -->

Once enough nodes do both, coordination stops depending primarily on hierarchy, consensus rituals, market coercion, or voting blocs. Those tools may still exist at the margins, but they are no longer carrying the whole burden.

The burden shifts back onto reality-contact and local fit.

**Theorem .** The coordination problem is not primarily a problem of missing common ground. It is a problem of one-sided expression and mediated capability. As nodes move toward equatorial balance on the shared sphere, coordination can emerge with sharply reduced coercive overhead.

1. By the operative geometry is a single shared sphere.
