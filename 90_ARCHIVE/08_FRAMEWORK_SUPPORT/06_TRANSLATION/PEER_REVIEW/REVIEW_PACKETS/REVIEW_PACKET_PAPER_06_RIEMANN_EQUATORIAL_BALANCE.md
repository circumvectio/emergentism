## 1. Introduction

The Riemann hypothesis (RH), formulated in 1859, states that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function

ζ(s) = Σ_{n=1}^{∞} n^{-s}

lie on the critical line Re(s) = 1/2 in the complex plane.

The hypothesis has resisted proof for over 165 years. It is widely considered the most important open problem in mathematics. Computational verification has confirmed billions of zeros on the critical line with no counterexamples.

This paper does not prove the Riemann hypothesis. This paper reframes it — proposing that the critical line's special status may be a consequence of a geometric principle (equatorial balance on S²) rather than an analytic accident. <!-- [C] -->

## 2. The Observation

### 2.1 The Critical Strip as a Sphere

The non-trivial zeros of ζ(s) lie in the critical strip:

0 < Re(s) < 1

This strip is bounded by Re(s) = 0 and Re(s) = 1. These are the two boundary values. On the Burri Sphere:

- Re(s) = 0 corresponds to the south pole (• = 0)
- Re(s) = 1 corresponds to the north pole as projected onto the strip
- Re(s) = 1/2 is the equator — the midpoint, the balance point

The Riemann hypothesis states that all non-trivial zeros sit at the equator of the critical strip.

### 2.2 The Equatorial Condition

On the Burri Sphere, the equator is characterized by:

- φ = ν = 1 (the two coordinates are balanced)
- B = sin θ = 1 (maximum balance)
- P∞ = φ · ν = 1 (the product is conserved)

The equator is the unique position of maximum balance. It is the Nash equilibrium of the sphere. It is the attractor toward which all balanced systems tend.

### 2.3 The Correspondence

If the critical strip is read as a sphere (compactifying 0 < Re(s) < 1 onto S¹, with the imaginary axis Im(s) running perpendicular), then:

- The "potential" P = Re(s) · (1 - Re(s)) is maximized at Re(s) = 1/2
- This is the analogue of P = P∞ = φ · ν = 1 with the substitution φ = Re(s), ν = 1 - Re(s)
- The product φ · ν = Re(s)(1 - Re(s)) achieves its maximum of 1/4 at Re(s) = 1/2

The critical line is the equator of the critical strip under the product Re(s) · (1 - Re(s)).
