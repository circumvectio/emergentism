# Irrational Numbers as Processes: A Philosophical Reframing via the Riemann Sphere

## Abstract

We propose a philosophical reframing of irrational numbers: rather than treating them as static, completed objects, we consider them as non-halting processes -- ongoing computations that never terminate when expressed in any base-N positional notation. This perspective is deeply aligned with Brouwer's intuitionism and with the philosophy of computation following Turing. We use the Riemann sphere $S^2$ as a geometric lens to illustrate -- not prove -- a key observation: every real number, rational or irrational, occupies a single finite point on $S^2$. The sphere does not *resolve* irrationality (which is an algebraic property invariant under geometric embedding), but it provides a framework in which the infinite decimal expansion of an irrational number is revealed as a property of the base-N representation system, not of the number itself. This paper is an interpretive essay in the philosophy of mathematics, not a structural proof. <!-- [I] -->

**Keywords:** irrational numbers, Riemann sphere, representation systems, Brouwer intuitionism, philosophy of mathematics, computable analysis

---

## 1. Introduction

The distinction between rational and irrational numbers is among the oldest in mathematics. The Pythagoreans discovered -- to their reported horror -- that $\sqrt{2}$ cannot be expressed as a ratio of integers. Since antiquity, this distinction has been treated as a fact about the numbers themselves: some quantities are rational, others are not, and that is the end of the matter.

We do not dispute this fact. Irrationality is an algebraic property: a number is irrational if and only if it cannot be expressed as $a/b$ for integers $a, b$ with $b \neq 0$. This property is *invariant* -- it does not change under geometric embedding, coordinate transformation, or projection. $\pi$ is irrational on the number line, on the Riemann sphere, and in every other mathematical context.

What we propose is a *philosophical reframing* of what this irrationality means. We argue that:

1. The infinite, non-repeating decimal expansion of an irrational number is a property of the **representation system** (base-N positional notation), not of the number itself.
2. Irrational numbers are better understood as **processes** (non-halting computations) rather than as static objects -- a view endorsed by Brouwer's intuitionism, constructive mathematics, and computable analysis.
3. The Riemann sphere $S^2$ provides a useful geometric illustration: on $S^2$, every real number -- rational or irrational -- is a single finite point. The sphere does not distinguish rationals from irrationals geometrically. <!-- [I] -->

This paper is an interpretive essay. It does not prove that irrationality is "merely" a representation artifact (it is not -- irrationality is a genuine algebraic property). Rather, it argues that the *infinity* associated with irrational numbers -- the non-terminating expansion -- tells us something about the limitations of discrete notation systems, and that this insight has philosophical value. <!-- [I] -->

## 2. The Representation Artifact

### 2.1 Infinite Expansion Is Base-Dependent

Consider $1/3$. In base 10, its expansion is infinite and repeating: <!-- [I] -->

$$1/3 = 0.333333...$$ <!-- [I] -->

In base 3, the same number has a terminating expansion:

$$1/3 = 0.1_3$$ <!-- [I] -->

The infinite expansion of $1/3$ in base 10 is not a property of the number $1/3$. It is a property of the *interaction* between the number and the base-10 representation system. The number itself is finite and exact: it is the unique solution to $3x = 1$. <!-- [I] -->

Now consider $\pi$. In base 10:

$$\pi = 3.14159265358979323846...$$

The expansion is infinite and non-repeating. Unlike $1/3$, no integer base $b$ will produce a terminating expansion of $\pi$ (this follows from its irrationality, and more strongly from its transcendence). But the *nature* of the phenomenon is the same: a finite, well-defined quantity produces an infinite representation when encoded in a discrete positional system. <!-- [I] -->

### 2.2 The Mismatch Between Discrete Notation and Continuous Structure

The infinite decimal representation of irrational numbers reflects an inherent mismatch between discrete base-N notation and the continuous structure of $\mathbb{R}$. Positional notation systems are built from a finite alphabet of digits. They can exactly represent only those numbers whose denominators (in lowest terms) are products of the base's prime factors. Everything else requires an infinite string.

For rational numbers, this infinite string eventually repeats -- the division algorithm enters a cycle. For irrational numbers, it never repeats -- there is no cycle to enter, because the number is not a ratio of integers. <!-- [I] -->

The key philosophical observation: the *infinity* of the expansion is a property of the **notation system's attempt to capture the number**, not a property of the number's intrinsic complexity. The number $\pi$ is finitely and exactly determined by its geometric definition (the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter). What is infinite is the *expression* of this quantity in base-N notation.

### 2.3 What the Sphere Illustrates

The Riemann sphere $S^2$ is the unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^3$, identified with the extended complex plane $\hat{\mathbb{C}} = \mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}$ via stereographic projection from the north pole $N = (0, 0, 1)$: <!-- [I] -->

$$z = \frac{x_1 + i x_2}{1 - x_3}$$

On $S^2$, every real number -- rational or irrational -- occupies a single finite point. The point corresponding to $\pi$ is no different in kind from the point corresponding to $3$ or $1/7$. The sphere does not distinguish rationals from irrationals geometrically; it provides a framework where each number is simply a location. <!-- [I] -->

This does *not* mean the sphere "resolves" irrationality. The algebraic property of being irrational is invariant under any embedding. $\pi$ remains irrational whether we place it on the number line, the sphere, or any other space. What the sphere illustrates is that the *infinity* associated with the decimal representation -- which is often conflated with the irrationality itself -- is a property of how we *write down* numbers, not of what numbers *are*.

## 3. Irrationals as Processes

### 3.1 The Computational Perspective

In computable analysis, a real number $x$ is represented not by a single finite string but by a *program* that produces successively better rational approximations to $x$. From this perspective:

- **Rational numbers** correspond to halting computations: the division algorithm terminates (or enters a detectable cycle), and the complete expansion can be finitely described.
- **Computable irrational numbers** ($\pi$, $e$, $\sqrt{2}$) correspond to non-halting computations: the program that produces successive digits runs forever. The expansion is never "complete." <!-- [I] -->

This is the sense in which irrationals are *processes* (verbs) rather than *objects* (nouns). The number $\pi$ is not a completed infinite decimal sitting somewhere in Platonic heaven. It is the *activity* of computing successive digits -- an activity that, by the nature of irrationality, never terminates. <!-- [I] -->

### 3.2 Kolmogorov Complexity: A Crucial Distinction

It is essential to distinguish between the complexity of the *description* and the complexity of the *output*.

Computable irrational numbers like $\pi$, $e$, and $\sqrt{2}$ have **finite** Kolmogorov complexity. There exist short programs that compute them: <!-- [I] -->

- "Compute $\pi$ via the Leibniz series" is a finite program.
- "Compute $\sqrt{2}$ by Newton's method starting from 1" is a finite program.
- "Compute $e$ via $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 1/n!$" is a finite program.

What is infinite is the **output**, not the **description**. The program is finite; the computation it specifies does not halt. This is a fundamental distinction that must not be conflated.

Only *uncomputable* numbers -- such as Chaitin's $\Omega$ (the halting probability) or specific Solovay-random reals -- have infinite Kolmogorov complexity in the sense that no finite program produces their digits. The vast majority of familiar irrationals ($\pi$, $e$, $\sqrt{2}$, $\phi$) are computable and finitely describable. <!-- [I] -->

### 3.3 The Hierarchy of Descriptive Complexity

With the above distinction in mind, we can describe a hierarchy -- not of Kolmogorov complexity, but of *computational behavior*:

**Level 0: Structural Constants** $\{0, 1, \infty\}$

The additive identity, the multiplicative identity, and the point at infinity on $S^2$. These are the scaffolding of arithmetic, not the results of computation.

**Level 1: Naturals and Integers**

Each natural number $n$ is the result of $n$ applications of the successor function. The computation halts in finitely many steps.

**Level 2: Rationals**

Each rational $p/q$ is the result of a division that either terminates or enters a finite cycle. The complete expansion is finitely describable.

**Level 3: Computable Irrationals**

Each computable irrational ($\pi$, $e$, $\sqrt{2}$, $\phi$) is finitely describable (finite Kolmogorov complexity) but its decimal expansion is the output of a non-halting computation. The description is finite; the expansion is infinite. <!-- [I] -->

**Level 4: Uncomputable Reals**

These have genuinely infinite Kolmogorov complexity: no finite program produces their digits. Almost all real numbers (in the measure-theoretic sense) are of this type.

This hierarchy describes computational *behavior*, not ontological *status*. On the Riemann sphere, numbers at every level occupy single, finite points. The hierarchy is a property of how these numbers interact with computational and representational systems.

## 4. The Sphere Does Not Explain Irrationality

### 4.1 An Important Limitation

The sphere mapping applies equally to ALL real numbers -- rational and irrational. The stereographic projection is a bijection between $S^2 \setminus \{N\}$ and $\mathbb{C}$. Every rational number maps to a point on $S^2$, and every irrational number maps to a point on $S^2$, and there is no geometric property of the sphere-side points that distinguishes one from the other. <!-- [I] -->

Therefore, the sphere does *not* explain irrationality specifically. It does not tell us *why* $\pi$ is irrational and $22/7$ is not. Irrationality is an algebraic/number-theoretic property that is entirely invisible to the sphere's geometry. <!-- [I] -->

### 4.2 What the Sphere DOES Illustrate

What the sphere provides is a *perspective shift* -- a context in which the dramatic difference between finite and infinite decimal expansions (which looms so large in base-N notation) disappears. On the sphere:

- $3.0$ is a point. <!-- [I] -->
- $3.14159265...$ is a point. <!-- [I] -->
- $0.333...$ is a point. <!-- [I] -->

All three are equally "finite" as geometric objects. The distinction between terminating, repeating, and non-repeating decimal expansions -- which drives the entire rational/irrational classification in positional notation -- has no geometric correlate on $S^2$.

This does not make irrationality less real. It suggests that the *phenomenology* of irrationality -- the experience of "a number that can never be fully written down" -- is a property of our notation, not of the number. The number can be finitely specified (as a geometric ratio, a convergent series, a root of an equation). What cannot be finitely expressed is its representation in any base-N system. <!-- [I] -->

## 5. Connection to Brouwer's Intuitionism

L.E.J. Brouwer (1881--1966) argued that mathematical objects do not exist independently of the constructive processes that generate them. In particular, Brouwer treated real numbers as *choice sequences* -- ongoing, never-completed processes of selecting digits or rational approximations. <!-- [I] -->

For Brouwer, $\pi$ is not a completed infinite decimal. It is the *process* of computing successive digits. The number is never "done." At any moment, we have computed $\pi$ to $n$ digits, but the full expansion remains in the future. <!-- [I] -->

This perspective is deeply aligned with the view developed here:

- Both the sphere perspective and the intuitionist perspective emphasize the **processual** nature of irrational numbers when represented in discrete notation.
- Both recognize that the "infinity" of irrational expansions reflects something about the *representational framework*, not about the underlying mathematical quantity.
- Both resist the Platonist picture of irrational numbers as completed infinite objects existing in an abstract realm.

This is an **interpretive parallel**, not a geometric derivation. Brouwer arrived at his conclusions through philosophical reflection on the nature of mathematical construction. We arrive at a similar conclusion through reflection on the geometry of the Riemann sphere. Neither argument constitutes a proof that irrationality is "merely" representational -- because irrationality is a genuine algebraic property. But both arguments suggest that the *phenomenological experience* of irrationality (the never-ending expansion) tells us as much about our representational tools as about the numbers themselves. <!-- [I] -->

---

## 6. The Cantor Diagonal: A Remark

Cantor's diagonal argument (1891) established that the real numbers are uncountable: no bijection exists between $\mathbb{N}$ and $\mathbb{R}$. We offer a brief interpretive remark, not a refutation.

The diagonal argument constructs, from any proposed enumeration of reals, a new real not in the enumeration. This is a valid proof within ZFC set theory, and we do not dispute it. However, we note that the argument operates entirely within the framework of infinite decimal representations. The "new" real is constructed digit by digit -- it is, in Brouwer's terms, a choice sequence.

On $S^2$, both $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ map to points. The sphere is a compact manifold regardless of the cardinality questions that arise on the number line. This does not invalidate Cantor's result (the sphere has uncountably many points too). But it does illustrate that cardinality is a set-theoretic property, not a geometric one. The "size" of the reals is a fact about the structure of $\mathbb{R}$ as an ordered field, not about the spatial nature of the numbers. <!-- [I] -->

---

## 7. Conclusion

We have argued for a philosophical reframing of irrational numbers, based on three observations:

1. **Irrationals are processes.** In computable analysis and Brouwer's intuitionism, irrational numbers are non-halting computations -- activities, not completed objects. This is the standard view in constructive mathematics, and we endorse it. Computable irrationals like $\pi$ have finite Kolmogorov complexity (the description is finite), but their decimal expansion is the output of a computation that never terminates. <!-- [I] -->

2. **The infinite expansion is a representation artifact.** The infinite, non-repeating decimal expansion of an irrational number reflects the mismatch between discrete base-N notation and the continuous structure of $\mathbb{R}$. This does not mean irrationality is a representation artifact -- irrationality is a genuine algebraic property. But the *infinity* of the expansion tells us about the limitations of positional notation, not about the intrinsic complexity of the number.

3. **The Riemann sphere provides a useful perspective.** On $S^2$, every real number is a single finite point. The sphere does not distinguish rationals from irrationals and does not "resolve" irrationality. But it illustrates that the infinite decimal expansion -- which so dominates our experience of irrational numbers -- is not an intrinsic feature of the numbers but a consequence of how we represent them. <!-- [I] -->

This paper is an interpretive essay, not a proof. Irrationality remains a genuine mathematical property. What we hope to have shown is that the *phenomenology* of irrationality -- the experience of a number that "never ends" -- is as much about us (our notation systems, our computational frameworks) as it is about the numbers themselves. <!-- [I] -->
