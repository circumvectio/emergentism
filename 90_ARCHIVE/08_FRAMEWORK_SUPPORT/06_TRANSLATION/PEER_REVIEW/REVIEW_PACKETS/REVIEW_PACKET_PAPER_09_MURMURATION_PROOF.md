## 2. The Starling Evidence

### 2.1 The Data

Ballerini et al. (2008) performed three-dimensional stereophotogrammetric reconstruction of large starling flocks (*Sturnus vulgaris*) over Rome. Using synchronized stereo cameras and computational triangulation, they determined the spatial position of every individual bird in flocks ranging from hundreds to thousands of individuals **[B]**.

The data quality was unprecedented: full 3D coordinates for each bird in multiple flock configurations, across multiple events. This was not a simulation. It was not a model. It was the actual geometry of actual flocks, measured to high precision.

### 2.2 The Key Finding: Topological, Not Metric

The central result: each starling interacts with a fixed NUMBER of neighbors (6--7), not all neighbors within a fixed DISTANCE **[B]**.

This distinction is critical. A metric interaction rule would say: "I pay attention to every bird within 3 meters." The number of interactants would vary with local density -- dense regions would have many connections, sparse regions few. A topological interaction rule says: "I pay attention to my 6 or 7 nearest neighbors, regardless of how far away they are." The number of interactants is fixed; what varies is the distance.

Ballerini et al. tested both models against the data. The topological model fit. The metric model did not **[B]**.

The implications are profound. Topological interaction means the flock's coherence is invariant under density changes. Whether the birds are packed tight or spread wide, each node maintains the same connectivity. The structure is count-based, not distance-based. The topology is preserved even as the geometry deforms.

### 2.3 The Number: 6--7

The specific number matters. Each bird tracks 6 to 7 nearest neighbors. Not 3. Not 15. Not 50. Six to seven.

This number has a geometric interpretation: the kissing number in two dimensions. The kissing number $k(d)$ is the maximum number of non-overlapping unit spheres that can simultaneously touch a central unit sphere in $d$ dimensions. In $d = 2$, $k(2) = 6$ exactly **[A]**: six equal circles can be arranged around a central circle such that each touches the center and its two neighbors. This is the hexagonal close-packing configuration -- the geometry of honeycombs, soap bubbles, and optimal sphere packing in the plane.

The starling's 6--7 neighbor count sits at this geometric boundary. Each bird is the center of its own hexagonal cluster. The flock, viewed from above, approximates a dynamic hexagonal mesh.

**The aggregate product is conserved.** The flock as a whole does not collapse into disorder (all $\nu$, no $\varphi$). Nor does it freeze into rigid formation (all $\varphi$, no $\nu$). ## 7. Why 6? A Candidate Derivation

### 7.1 The Coordination-Cost Argument

The cost of coordination between $N$ nodes grows with the number of pairwise connections: $N(N-1)/2$. This is the Brooks law insight -- communication channels grow quadratically with team size **[B]**.

The benefit of adding nodes grows linearly, modulated by the balance function:

$$\text{Net benefit} = N \cdot B(\theta) - \lambda \cdot \frac{N(N-1)}{2}$$

where $B(\theta) = \sin\theta$ is the balance function from the Burri Sphere (maximized at the equator, zero at the poles) and $\lambda$ is the coordination cost per pairwise connection.

The optimal $N$ is found by setting the marginal benefit equal to the marginal cost:

$$\frac{d}{dN}\left[N \sin\theta - \lambda \frac{N(N-1)}{2}\right] = 0$$

$$\sin\theta - \lambda(N - \frac{1}{2}) = 0$$

$$N_{\text{optimal}} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\lambda} + \frac{1}{2}$$

At the equator ($\theta = \pi/2$, $\sin\theta = 1$): <!-- [C] -->

$$N_{\text{optimal}} = \frac{1}{\lambda} + \frac{1}{2}$$

For $N_{\text{optimal}} \approx 6$, we need $\lambda \approx 0.18$. <!-- [C] -->

### 7.2 The Geometric Bound

This derivation requires a specific value of $\lambda$ and is therefore framework-dependent **[C]**. However, there is a framework-INDEPENDENT geometric bound that does not depend on any cost function:

The kissing number in 2D is exactly 6 **[A]**. You cannot have more than 6 equatorial neighbors in two-dimensional space. This is a theorem of Euclidean geometry, not a parameter of any model. Regardless of what $\lambda$ is, regardless of the cost function, regardless of the benefit structure, you CANNOT pack more than 6 equal-size coordination partners around a central node in 2D.

The argument is: the coordination geometry of organisms operating in a plane (birds in a flock, soldiers on a battlefield, players on a court) is effectively two-dimensional. The kissing number provides a hard geometric ceiling on the number of equatorial neighbors. The empirical observation that optimal group size clusters at 5--7 is consistent with systems operating near this ceiling.

### 7.3 Research Direction
