# The Lived Compass

> See the actual situation. Price the model. Name the possible futures. Count
> the means. Keep every bearer visible. Commit one act. Receive the world's
> answer. Change the map.

This is the minimum practical form of Emergentism. It does not require belief
in W0, strong emergence, the five-force correspondence, a quantum
interpretation, or the Titan imagery. A person may reject every metaphysical
wager and still use the discipline.

## 1. The seven questions

Before a consequential action, ask:

1. **Actuality — What is happening?** Separate observed facts, reports,
   interpretations and missing information.
2. **Model — What am I assuming?** Name the causal story, confidence, strongest
   rival and evidence that would change it.
3. **Possibility — Which futures can I presently represent?** Include doing
   nothing, delay, reversible trials and options proposed by affected people.
4. **Means — What can actually be done?** Count time, body, skill, money,
   tools, authority, cooperation and physical constraints.
5. **Justice — Who acts, benefits, pays and bears risk?** Include hidden,
   future, ecological and absent bearers where materially affected.
6. **Commitment — What is the smallest responsible act?** Prefer reversible,
   informative action when uncertainty is high.
7. **Receipt — What happened, and what must change?** Record both expected and
   actual consequences without letting intention manufacture the outcome.

These questions are a compass, not a calculator. No single score can answer
them all.

## 2. The typed Soul Loop

```text
territory X_t
  → fallible actual model M_t
  → represented alternatives Ω_t
  → ranking under declared values U_t
  → selection using actual means V_t
  → commitment receipt q_t
  → world transition and outcome receipt r_(t+1)
  → update of model and selector
```

Three gaps must remain inspectable:

| Gap | Comparison | Typical failure |
|---|---|---|
| cognitive | territory versus model | confidence exceeds contact |
| execution | intention versus performed act | means, skill or authorization fail |
| outcome | expected versus received consequence | world and other agents answer differently |

A loop is corrective when declared error falls. A reinforcing loop may amplify
accuracy or delusion; “positive” and “negative” feedback are dynamical signs,
not moral labels.

## 3. Foresight and means

`Φ` is a present assessment of how well an actual model represents and ranks
relevant alternatives. `V` is present usable means. `P_node=ΦV` is one declared
AND-class model: a zero in either factor blocks effective enactment inside that
model.

Do not infer more than it says:

- high `Φ` does not make an imagined future true;
- high `V` does not make force wise or authorized;
- the product is not a universal metric of persons;
- `Φ=1` forces `V=0` only under a separately measured finite-resource budget;
- the world and other agents still determine the outcome.

Use the formula as a diagnostic question—*is the failure primarily in the map,
the means, or their coupling?*—and compare it with simpler rivals when making
predictions.

## 4. The Justice envelope

For consequential action, make this record explicit:

```text
principal             whose purposes are represented
mandate               what authorizes the act
scope                  what may and may not be changed
consent                who agreed, refused, or could not consent
custody                who holds affected resources and records
expiry_or_revocation   when permission ends and how it is withdrawn
contest_path           how an affected bearer challenges the act
actor                  who actually performs it
consequence_bearers    everyone materially exposed to the outcome
payer                  who bears cost
beneficiary            who receives value
reversibility          what can be restored
exit                   how a bearer can leave
```

Authorization is not truth. A permitted action can be mistaken. A true
observation does not by itself authorize intervention.

## 5. Ordinary decisions

For a reversible, low-stakes decision:

1. write the choice in one sentence;
2. list the actual evidence and one serious alternative explanation;
3. list at least three options, including no action;
4. identify the missing means and affected bearers;
5. choose the smallest action that can teach you something;
6. define beforehand what result counts as success, failure or ambiguity;
7. act, record, and update.

If the cost of recording exceeds the consequence, use a one-line receipt. The
discipline should not become bureaucracy that consumes the value it protects.

## 6. Tragic decisions

Sometimes every available action harms someone or closes a valuable future.
Strict syntropy is then unavailable. Do not relabel the least-bad option as
win–win.

Use the tragic protocol:

1. state plainly that no harm-free option is known;
2. widen the search and invite affected bearers to propose alternatives;
3. protect non-negotiable rights and bodily safety;
4. minimize irreversible and concentrated harm;
5. distribute burdens transparently rather than onto the least powerful;
6. preserve contest, compensation, repair and exit where possible;
7. name who accepts responsibility;
8. record the residue—the harm that remains even after the best available act.

The residue must not disappear from the success story.

## 7. Sacrifice

Sacrifice is a costly act in which one bearer voluntarily accepts loss for
another bearer, a relationship, a value or a future. It is not strict syntropy
when the sacrificer's durable potential falls.

A collective may honor voluntary sacrifice but may not demand it as proof of
loyalty or ethics. Test:

- Was consent informed and revocable?
- Who defined the sacred objective?
- Who benefits?
- Could a less costly act achieve the same end?
- What becomes irreversible?
- Is refusal punished materially or spiritually?

Hidden coercion converts sacrifice into extraction.

## 8. Conflict

In conflict, do not begin by assigning permanent identities such as god,
demon, oppressor or victim. Begin with receiptable moves and consequences.

1. state each side's account in terms they recognize;
2. separate disputed facts from disputed values;
3. identify asymmetric means, risks and exit capacity;
4. test whether one gain requires an invisible loss elsewhere;
5. distinguish protection, retaliation, punishment and revenge;
6. prefer boundaries that stop harm while preserving future revision;
7. use force only under lawful authorization, necessity and proportionality;
8. update the moral classification when the facts change.

The framework does not guarantee reconciliation. Sometimes Justice requires a
firm boundary against an uncorrected extractor.

## 9. Collective traces

For a family, organization, institution or movement, inspect the trace field:

- What behavior is remembered and rewarded?
- What gets erased?
- Does the pattern survive personnel turnover?
- Does changing the trace change later choices?
- Whose labor maintains it?
- Which objective-like bias recurs?
- What are the visible substrate and human costs?

Only the full intervention-sensitive pattern licenses the term
**Egregoreotype**. Awe, popularity, branding or longevity alone does not.

## 10. When to stop

Stop the loop when further analysis costs more than the reversible decision,
when the evidence has reached the declared threshold, or when delay itself is
the largest harm. Do not confuse endless modeling with wisdom.

Put the framework down when:

- its vocabulary obscures the actual problem;
- a simpler method works better;
- the person affected asks you to stop applying it to them;
- the receipts show no improvement;
- it becomes an identity you must defend; or
- it narrows the options it promised to widen.

## 12. The practice in one breath

> Do not ask the model to guarantee the future. Ask it to expose the present
> assumptions, widen the honest alternatives, couple one choice to lawful
> means, keep every bearer visible, and make the consequence capable of
> correcting you.

**Boundary:** this practice is not medical, legal, financial or clinical
advice. In those domains it supplements—not replaces—qualified professional
judgment, applicable law, informed consent and emergency procedures.
