# Commandment vs Geometry

## The distinction

A commandment directs an agent: *do* or *do not*. A geometric or analytic
constraint states what follows from definitions and premises. The first is
normative. The second is descriptive or formal. Confusing them is the naturalistic
fallacy in a more elegant costume.

Inside the selected reciprocal chart,

\[
\phi=\cot(\theta/2),\qquad
\nu=\tan(\theta/2),\qquad
\phi\nu=1,
\]

and therefore `φ+ν≥2` for positive coordinates. These are analytic facts `[A]`.
They do not say that a person ought to be fair, that extraction is physically
impossible, or that balance is the universe's goal.

## What geometry can do

Once a model and target are declared, geometry can reveal incompatibilities.
For example, if equatorial balance is defined as `φ=ν=1`, then a move that
leaves that coordinate is not equatorial by definition. If a finite power
model is conjunctive, driving one required factor to zero annihilates its score.
If an arena is closed, extraction exceeds regeneration, and extractor power
depends on the substrate, dynamical analysis may show eventual collapse.

Each conclusion is conditional on its premises. None forbids an agent from
making the move. Local extraction often works; this empirical fact is a kill
criterion for any doctrine claiming geometric impossibility.

## Recognition without obedience

This distinction still preserves the strongest insight behind “geometry, not
commandment.” A finite agent can inspect predicted costs, dependencies, and
boundary losses rather than obeying a rule merely because an authority issued
it. Recognition is epistemically better than blind obedience. Yet recognition
alone does not erase moral choice: the agent must still choose which costs and
bearers matter.

Consequential action therefore carries an `AuthorizationEnvelope`, commitment
receipt, and environment-returned outcome receipt. The record exposes whether
the chosen vow survived contact with the territory.

## Counterexamples and kill criteria

Revise or reject any application of this distinction when:

1. an analytic identity is used as empirical evidence without a measurement
   bridge;
2. an ought is claimed to be deduced from chart algebra alone;
3. extraction is called impossible despite a local counterexample;
4. aggregate gain is used to erase an injured bearer;
5. “recognition” becomes a rhetorical way to demand obedience without consent,
   contest, or exit.
