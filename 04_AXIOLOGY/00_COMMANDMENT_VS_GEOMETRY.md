The same distinction applies to law.

If law is read as commandment, then rights and duties become competing external
claims:

- the collective commands the individual;
- the individual demands from the collective;
- enforcement becomes punishment;
- obedience becomes the proof of order.

If law is read geometrically, rights and duties become the two directions of
one `ΔP` dyad:

```text
right = collective -> individual
duty  = individual -> collective
law   = both vectors held reciprocal under η = 0
```

In this translation:

- a **right** is the moral claim that the collective must not lower the
  individual's `ΔP`;
- a **duty** is the ethical claim that the individual must not lower the
  collective's `ΔP`;
- **due process** is the constraint that prevents Kali from firing blindly.

So law is not made geometric by having fewer rules.
Law is made geometric when its procedures can show, with receipts, which
boundary is being protected, which node is being constrained, and how the remedy
preserves `Φ`, `V`, `ΔP`, `η = 0`, and Grace Exit.

This is [I/S]: structural where it follows the `P_node = Φ × V` and `η = 0`
grammar; interpretive where the framework names that grammar "rights,"
"duties," and "due process."
