# The Extraction Law

> **The one line.** Extraction is a gain to one bearer purchased through an
> unauthorized or concealed durable loss to another. It can be profitable,
> stable, or long-lived. Its wrongness in Emergentism comes from the explicit
> Justice postulate—not from a promise that nature always punishes it.

## 1. Formal definition

Let `b` be a beneficiary, `p` a payer, `q` a commitment receipt, `r` an outcome
receipt, and `J^R` the receipted Justice test. Define:

\[
\operatorname{Extraction}(q,r;b,p)
\iff
\Delta_T^R W_b(q,r)>0
\land\Delta_T^R W_p(q,r)<0
\land\neg\operatorname{AuthorizedCost}^R(q,r;p,b).
\]

`AuthorizedCost^R` entails the complete receipted Justice predicate and specific
cost consent. Its failure may be lack of consent, hidden cost, invalid
authorization, capture, loss of custody, absent contest, imposed
irreversibility, or an unaccountable consequence bearer.

This definition is bilateral. It does not ask whether

\[
\sum_x \Delta_T^R W_x>0.
\]

That aggregate is descriptively useful but ethically insufficient. A gain to
many cannot by itself compensate for destroying one bearer. The payer,
beneficiary, authorization, and receipt stay visible.

## 2. Extraction can work

The framework must not deny the obvious:

- theft can enrich a thief;
- rent can enrich its collector;
- conquest can enrich a state;
- predation can feed a predator;
- attention capture can grow an institution;
- one-shot transfer in the illustrative coupling model raises the extractor's
  payoff when `0<λ<1`.

Power-Max preserves the exact counterexample. For normalized factors, fixed
`Φ_i>0`, and `0<ΔV≤min(V_j,1−V_i)`, transferring `ΔV` from `j` to `i`
leaves the network mean unchanged and gives

\[
\Delta P_{\mathrm{eff}}(i)=\Phi_i(1-\lambda)\Delta V>0.
\]

Coupling proves interdependence in that model, not automatic cooperation.
Justice excludes the transfer because of what it does to `j`, not because the
extractor necessarily loses.

## 3. Dynamics are empirical, not moral proofs

Extraction may degrade its substrate and later harm the extractor. Predator–
prey cycles, resistance, institutional distrust, depletion, and trophic losses
are important candidate mechanisms. But their presence, strength, horizon, and
boundary are empirical questions.

No universal theorem licenses:

```text
extraction ⇒ inevitable collapse
injustice ⇒ eventual defeat
sustainability ⇒ justice
natural selection ⇒ moral legitimacy
```

An extractive arrangement may persist when the payer is replenished, replaced,
silenced, externalized, or too weak to contest. Conversely, ecological
predation can be dynamically sustainable without becoming a moral relation.
Nature supplies models and consequences, not the Justice premise.

The empirical question for a system is therefore conditional:

> Given a named extraction rate, renewal process, coupling, horizon, and
> boundary, what happens to each bearer and to the shared substrate?

The answer may support intervention, but it never makes an unconsenting payer
disappear from the moral account.

## 4. `η=0` is a normative and institutional gate

`η=0` means that hidden rent or extraction is excluded at the declared
boundary. It is part of the Justice envelope `[I]` and must be made operational:

- expose payer and beneficiary;
- verify mandate, scope, consent, custody, and contest;
- price physical and option-cone costs honestly;
- separate commitment from outcome;
- refuse aggregate compensation for a destroyed bearer;
- make exit, revocation, and repair real.

This is neither a geometric impossibility nor a law that all natural systems
obey. It is a constitutional design choice justified by the declared good of
Beauty, Truth, and Justice.

## 5. Taking is not always extraction

An act involving transfer, consumption, constraint, or destruction is not
classified by appearance alone. Distinguish:

| Class | Conditions |
|---|---|
| reciprocal exchange | valid authorization/consent; both receipted durable changes nonnegative |
| restitution | proportionate return of wrongfully held value under due process |
| protective constraint | valid scope, evidence, proportionality, receipt, review, and contest |
| ecological predation | biological interaction; moral classification requires a separately justified agency boundary |
| voluntary sacrifice | payer knowingly accepts a durable loss; separate costly class |
| extraction | beneficiary rises, payer falls, Justice fails |

Calling a victim "substrate," "defector," "counterfeit," or "the weak" does not
license taking. The evidence and Authorization envelope must do the work.

## 7. Decision test

For a suspected extraction, ask:

1. Who is the beneficiary and who is the payer?
2. What are the baseline, horizon, and separate receipted deltas?
3. What was authorized, by whom, for what scope, and with what consent?
4. Who controls custody and who bears the consequence?
5. Can the payer contest, revoke, repair, or exit?
6. Is a positive aggregate hiding a negative bearer?
7. Is a claim of future repayment supported by a model and later receipt?
8. Is "nature," "efficiency," "the collective," or "necessity" being used to
   erase a person or cost?

When payer or receipt is unknown, classification fails closed.

---

## 8. Kill and revision criteria

Revise this application when:

- a purported payer loss is not measurable or is contradicted by receipts;
- consent and authorization are demonstrably valid and no hidden durable loss
  remains;
- the selected `W` measure reverses under a better validated alternative;
- the boundary was chosen after the fact;
- a protective or restitutive act is mislabeled extraction despite passing due
  process and bilateral accounting;
- the framework predicts substrate collapse that does not occur under the
  stated horizon and renewal dynamics.

The normative definition remains a declared Emergentist commitment, not an
empirical theorem. Its honesty lies in showing exactly where the value premise
enters.
