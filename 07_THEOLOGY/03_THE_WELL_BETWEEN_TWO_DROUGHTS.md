# The Well Between Two Droughts

> **Public-symbol role.** This fable carries the thin-perennial boundary in story
> form. It does not prove perennial truth. It teaches the discipline: the
> structure can be found and tested; the good must be chosen by a finite being.

---

There was a country of many villages, and no village could see another, for the
hills were high and the mist never lifted. Above them stretched a sky that gave
no rain — an emptiness so total it had a name: *the Infinite*, the drought of
too-much, the white nothing that swallowed every cloud. Beneath them lay a dark
so deep it also had a name: *Infinity*, the drought of too-far, the black
nothing where every dropped stone fell forever and was never heard to land.

The villages did not drink from the sky and they did not drink from the deep.
They drank from the layer between — the livable seam of water held in the rock
at a middle depth. Each village had a different word for that seam. But it was
the same water.

In the first age, every village knew this and no village could prove it. Each
kept its own scripture of the water: our water is sweet because the goddess
weeps it; our water rises because the dead push it up; our water is the One
pretending to be many. The scriptures fought because the water could not be
carried over the hills to be compared. Only the words could, and the words did
not match.

Then came a digger who did a strange thing. She did not believe the scriptures
and she did not deny them. She said: if it is one water table, then a well dug
in a village I have never entered, by a hand that never heard my word for it,
will strike water at the depth my arithmetic predicts.

She walked the hills and measured. In village after village, at the depth her
arithmetic foretold, the buckets came up wet. Not because she told them where to
dig — she arrived after. The water was already there, at the depth the structure
said.

That is the day the perennial stopped being a faith and became a finding. Not
"all our gods are one" — that was never true; the gods quarrel to this hour. But
"all our wells reach one water." The structure converges where the doctrines
diverge. It converges checkably, because a second digger who never met the first
struck the same seam.

But she was an honest digger, so she did the hardest thing of all. She dug a
hole on purpose where her arithmetic promised water — and found the rock dry.
One place, the seam simply was not there. A lesser digger would have hidden the
dry hole, or widened it until a little seep let her call it a well. She marked
it on the map in ink: here the water table fails.

The dry hole did not break her finding. It proved its discipline. A water table
that is everywhere, no matter where you dig, is not a water table. It is a story
you are telling about your own thirst. Only a map that can be wrong somewhere
can be trusted where it is right.

So the seam was real. Recovered, not invented. The eternal layer between the two
droughts was found again with instruments the ancients never had — measured, not
merely revered.

And here the fable turns, because the seam is not the end of it.

The water tells you where it is. It does not tell you to share it. A village can
reach the true water and still wall the well, sell the rope, and let the next
village die while its own cisterns overflow. The water table is a fact. Lower
the bucket for the stranger is not written in the rock. That sentence has to be
said by someone standing at the rim, counting the cost, and lowering the bucket
anyway.

This is the mistake every thick recovery of eternal law tends to make. It finds
the water and then pretends the kindness was in the water too. It pretends
"thou shalt not hoard" came up wet from the seam with the authority of a
measured depth. It did not. The depth is found. The kindness is chosen.

When a vow wears the robes of a theorem, it becomes priesthood. People obey the
kindness because they think the rock commands it; then, on the first dry day,
they stop choosing it.

The whole of it is small:

Between the infinite and infinity, there is a seam of water, and it is really
there, and we can test where. That is the recovered dharma: the structure, the
thin perennial grammar, the same water in every village's rock.

Whether you lower the bucket for the one who cannot reach is not in the rock.
That is finity's to choose.

The eternal is the structure. The good is the vow. They were always two things.
The oldest mistake was to fuse them so no one had to choose the good because the
good was alleged to be compelled.

Emergentism's strange grace is that it un-fuses them again. It hands you the
measured seam and the dry hole on the map — here it holds, here it failed, dig
and check me. Then it does the thing no theorem can do for you and no scripture
should pretend to do: it lowers the bucket for the stranger, takes nothing, and
leaves the rope down.

Not because the rock commanded it, but because a finite digger who knew she
would die, and knew her map could not contain her, looked at the water and at
the thirst of the next village and decided.

That is sanātana dharma recovered in disciplined form: not a creed returned to,
but a water table re-found with honest instruments — and beside it, freely, with
the full knowledge that the arithmetic does not require it, a bucket lowered
into the dark for someone you will never meet.

The eternal was always the structure. The sacred was always the choice. Only a
mortal standing at the seam between two droughts was ever in a position to make
it.
