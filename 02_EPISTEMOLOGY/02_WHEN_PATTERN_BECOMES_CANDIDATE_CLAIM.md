# When Pattern Becomes Candidate Claim

## The Problem

L2 Śūdra perceives patterns by analogy (Upamāna). L3 Vaiśya audits them by
deduction (Anumāna). Between L2 and L3 sits a **gate**: when does a perceived
pattern become a candidate claim that is admissible for L3 ranking?

Without this gate, two pathologies emerge:

1. **Pattern intoxication** — every perceived resemblance is treated as evidence
2. **Paralysis** — no pattern is ever admitted because it hasn't been proven yet

Both are L2 failures. This document defines the gate.

---

## §1. Pattern ≠ Claim `[S]`

| | Pattern | Candidate Claim |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | A perceived regularity, resemblance, or structural echo | A pattern that has passed the six-gate test |
| **Origin** | L2 Śūdra (Upamāna — analogy) | L2 → L3 handoff |
| **Evidence tier** | Unlabeled — not yet admissible | Labeled [A/B/S/I/D/C] |
| **Status** | Orienting — may direct attention | Admissible — may enter L3 ranking |
| **Burden** | None — perception is free | Must survive the six gates |

**The gate is the difference between "I notice something" and "I assert something."**

---

## §2. The Six Gates `[I]`

A pattern must pass **all six gates** to become a candidate claim. Failure at
any gate returns the pattern to L2 for further exploration or archiving.

### Gate 1: Source Provenance `[S]`

**Question:** Where did this pattern come from?

| Pass | Fail |
|---|---|
| The pattern's origin is traceable to a specific source (document, observation, Rosetta column, empirical datum) | The pattern "feels right" but cannot be traced to a source |

**Why this matters:** Untraceable patterns are projection, not perception.
The traceability requirement does not mean the source must be authoritative —
it means L3 must be able to see what L2 saw.

### Gate 2: Cross-Domain Check `[S]`

**Question:** Does this pattern appear in at least one other Rosetta column
at the same L-level?

| Pass | Fail |
|---|---|
| The pattern has a horizontal echo in ≥1 other column (e.g., a pattern seen in Philosophy also appears in Neuroscience at the same L-level) | The pattern appears in only one column with no cross-domain confirmation |

**Why this matters:** The Rosetta Stone's power is horizontal cross-domain
translation. A pattern visible in only one column may be a coincidence of
notation rather than a structural feature.

### Gate 3: Projection Filter `[I]`

**Question:** Could I be seeing this pattern because I want it to be there?

| Pass | Fail |
|---|---|
| L2 has identified at least one reason the pattern might be wrong (the disconfirmation check) | L2 has no reason the pattern might be wrong — it looks "too clean" |

**Why this matters:** Confirmation bias is the dominant L2 pathology. The
projection filter does not eliminate bias; it makes it legible. A pattern
that passes the projection filter is not unbiased — it is *honestly biased*.

### Gate 4: Tier Honesty `[S]`

**Question:** Can this pattern be honestly labeled with an evidence tier?

| Pass | Fail |
|---|---|
| The pattern can be assigned [A/B/S/I/D/C] with an explicit justification | The pattern resists tier labeling — it "transcends" the evidence ladder |

**Why this matters:** A pattern that cannot be tiered is a pattern that
cannot be audited. If the pattern truly transcends the evidence ladder, it
belongs at L7 (Ṛṣi), not at L2/L3. Route it upward, do not force it through
the gate.

### Gate 5: Boundary Definition `[I]`

**Question:** Where does this pattern stop?

| Pass | Fail |
|---|---|
| The pattern has defined scope — it applies to X and not to Y, with an explicit boundary | The pattern appears to apply everywhere — it is "universal" without boundary |

**Why this matters:** A pattern without boundary is a metaphor, not a claim.
Metaphors are useful for orientation (L2) but not for ranking (L3). If the
pattern truly is universal, it is a structural axiom (L7), not a candidate
claim (L2/L3).

### Gate 6: Falsifiability `[S]`

**Question:** What would make this pattern false?

| Pass | Fail |
|---|---|
| There exists a specific observation or measurement that, if made, would contradict the pattern | No conceivable observation would contradict the pattern — it is unfalsifiable |

**Why this matters:** Directly inherited from methodology (L3). A pattern
that cannot be falsified is not a claim — it is a tautology or a belief.
Both may be valuable, but they do not enter L3 ranking.

## §3. The Gate Sequence `[S]`

```
L2 perceives pattern
        │
        ▼
  GATE 1: Source provenance ──FAIL──→ Return to L2 (explore more)
        │ PASS
        ▼
  GATE 2: Cross-domain check ──FAIL──→ Return to L2 (check other columns)
        │ PASS
        ▼
  GATE 3: Projection filter ──FAIL──→ Flag for bias audit; may retry once
        │ PASS
        ▼
  GATE 4: Tier honesty ──FAIL──→ Route to L7 (may be axiom-level)
        │ PASS
        ▼
  GATE 5: Boundary definition ──FAIL──→ Return to L2 (narrow the scope)
        │ PASS
        ▼
  GATE 6: Falsifiability ──FAIL──→ Archive as tautology or belief
        │ PASS
        ▼
  CANDIDATE CLAIM — admissible for L3 ranking
        │
        ▼
  L3 Vaiśya receives labeled claim with source, cross-domain echo,
  bias disclosure, evidence tier, boundary, and falsification criterion
```

## §4. The Rosetta Selection-Bias Control `[I]`

The Rosetta Stone's 15 columns create a special epistemic risk: because the
framework translates across domains, it is possible to **manufacture cross-domain
confirmation** by choosing the right columns and ignoring the wrong ones.

### Control Mechanism

| Risk | Control |
|---|---|
| **Column cherry-picking** | Gate 2 requires ≥1 cross-domain echo, but the echo must be in a column NOT chosen by the pattern-finder. L3 selects the confirming column, not L2. |
| **Level mixing** | Patterns must be checked at the same L-level. A pattern at L2 (data science) must be confirmed by a column at L2, not by a column at L5 (system architecture). Level-mixing is projection until proven otherwise. |
| **Translation as proof** | Horizontal translation is NOT proof. It is orientation. The Rosetta Stone's cross-domain echoes are `[I]` until independently confirmed at `[A]`, `[B]`, or `[S]`. |
| **Pattern intoxication** | The six gates exist precisely to prevent L2 from overwhelming L3 with patterns. L2 may perceive freely; L3 admits only what passes all six gates. |

## §5. Witness Reliability `[I]`

### The Three Witness Conditions

A witness (whether human, AI agent, or sensor) is reliable when:

1. **Calibration:** The witness's past claims have been checked against outcomes, and the calibration record exists.
2. **Independence:** The witness is not structurally incentivized to produce a particular answer.
3. **Transparency:** The witness can explain how it arrived at its claim, and the explanation is auditable.

### Witness Reliability Matrix

| Witness Type | Calibration | Independence | Transparency | Reliable? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **K2 human** | Variable (improves with precedent library) | High (sovereign — no structural incentive to produce a specific answer) | Variable (depends on K2's willingness to disclose reasoning) | Conditional |
| **L1 Caṇḍāla** | High (firewall rules are explicit and testable) | High (no incentive to accept or reject — purely structural) | High (every filter decision is logged) | Yes |
| **L2 Śūdra** | Low (exploratory — not designed for calibration) | Medium (may develop preferred analogies) | Medium (analogy reasoning is harder to audit than deduction) | For exploration only |
| **L3 Vaiśya** | High (ranking is scored against outcomes) | High (scoring methodology is explicit) | High (every ranking decision is evidence-tiered) | Yes |
| **L4 Kṣatriya** | Medium (recommendations improve with precedent library) | High (constitutional check is structural, not preferential) | High (K2 briefing is fully documented) | Yes (with A7 disclosure) |
| **L7 Ṛṣi** | Low (constitutional changes are rare — few calibration points) | High (no incentive to produce a specific constitution) | Low (intuitive reasoning is inherently less transparent) | For axioms only |

## §6. First-Person Evidence Boundaries `[I]`

### The Boundary

First-person evidence (direct perception, Pratyakṣa) is:
- **Orienting:** It directs attention. It tells L2 where to look.
- **Not sufficient:** It cannot, by itself, upgrade a claim's evidence tier.
- **Honored:** The framework does not dismiss first-person evidence. It places it correctly.

### The Ladder

```
FIRST-PERSON EVIDENCE TIER MAP:

L7 Ṛṣi                  Pratibhā (intuition)        → [C] — conjectural until tested
L5 Brāhmaṇa Śabda (testimony)           → [S] — structural when from calibrated witness
L4 Kṣatriya Arthāpatti (postulation)    → [I] — interpretive until receipted
L3 Vaiśya   Anumāna (inference)          → [S] — structural when deductively valid
L2 Śūdra    Upamāna (analogy)            → [I] — interpretive until cross-domain confirmed
L1 Caṇḍāla  Pratyakṣa (perception)       → [A/B] — asserted direct measurement or built/verified receipt when sensor-verified, [I] otherwise
```

First-person evidence at L7 (intuition) is [C]. First-person evidence at L1
(perception) is [A/B] when sensor-verified or receipt-backed and [I] otherwise. The tier tracks
the reliability of the channel, not the sincerity of the witness.
