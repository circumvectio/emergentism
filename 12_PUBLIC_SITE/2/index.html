01
The Boundary

Where Flat Approximation Fails

The μ-limit is the boundary between dimensional levels. It is the point where a Dn structure's flat approximation fails, where the curvature of the sphere becomes detectable, and where the transition to D(n+1) occurs.

Notation μ(P→F) means "the μ-limit from Possibility (D5) to Factuality (D4)." The limit operator lim[δt→0] is the instantaneous boundary — not a process that takes time, but the limit of infinitely fast transition. The physical analog is quantum collapse: the Born rule, the measurement, the instant when superposition becomes definite.

At D2 the μ-limit is simpler: it is the point where the multiplicative ray from D1, extended toward infinity, does not stop but re-enters from the opposite side. The one-dimensional line has reached its own boundary and must become two-dimensional.

02
Stereographic Return

On the Riemann Sphere the Ray Re-emerges

On the Riemann sphere S² ≅ ℂℙ¹, stereographic projection places the number one at 45° — exactly halfway between the south pole (zero) and the north pole (infinity). When the ray reaches the pole it does not terminate. It re-emerges on the other side, and the one-dimensional line opens into a two-dimensional plane.

This is projective completion: zero and infinity are regular points, the south and north poles respectively, and every other number lives on the surface between them. The flat line is a local chart; the sphere is the completed object. The μ-limit of D2 is the moment when the chart is recognised as local and the global surface becomes visible.

The return is not a contradiction. It is the topological fact that a compact surface has no boundary. The ray that seemed to escape to infinity was always on the surface; it only appeared to leave because the chart was flat.

03
Dimensional Transition

D2 Is the Bridge from Line to Surface

In the dimensional scaffold (D0 → D6), D2 is the transition level. D1 is the number line: succession, ratio, and reciprocal relation. D3 is the sphere: closure, form, and persistent pattern. D2 is the bridge between them.

Time (D2) gives succession. The μ-limit is the principle that succession, pushed to its boundary, does not stop but transforms. A sequence that grows without bound does not end; it changes dimension. This is the geometric content of the claim that "D2 forces D3": a line extended to its limit must become a surface.

Strong emergence reads upward: the local structure of the line (D1) generates the global structure of the sphere (D3) when saturation is reached. Weak emergence reads downward: once D3 stabilises, it constrains and organises the temporal flow of D2. A melody (D3 form) organises a sequence of notes (D2 time).
