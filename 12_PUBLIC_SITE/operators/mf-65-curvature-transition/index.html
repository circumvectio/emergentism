MF-65: The Curvature Unification

From Mixed to Constant: Why the D5 Model Reads as Reconvergence

ABSTRACT

The torus T² has Gaussian curvature that varies across its surface: positive on the outer equator, negative on the inner equator, zero on the top and bottom circles. The sphere S² has constant positive Gaussian curvature everywhere. The genus transition (T² → S²) is therefore not merely a topological change (hole closes) but a curvature unification: mixed, sign-varying curvature becomes uniform, single-sign curvature.

This has a direct geodesic consequence inside the surface model. On positive-curvature regions, nearby geodesics tend to converge; on negative-curvature regions, nearby geodesics tend to diverge. On the torus, some geodesics converge and some diverge depending on where they are. On the sphere, great-circle geodesics reconverge in the closed geometry. The causal-horizon and agent-separation language is the framework's D4/D5 interpretation, not a theorem about minds or spacetime. [A for surface geometry; I for D-mapping]

The genus change can therefore model systemic awareness (D5) as the register in which apparently separate trajectories are read through a reconvergent whole. D4 permits permanent divergence in the analogy; D5 names the closed, reconvergent model. This gives a curvature reading of the Atman=Brahman identity (MF-44); it is not an empirical proof that all agents literally reconverge. [I/S]

I. GAUSSIAN CURVATURE

1.1 Definition

The Gaussian curvature K at a point p on a surface measures how the surface deviates from flatness. [A]

  • K > 0: Surface curves like a sphere. Nearby geodesics converge.
  • K = 0: Surface is locally flat. Nearby geodesics stay parallel.
  • K < 0: Surface curves like a saddle. Nearby geodesics diverge.

1.2 Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

For any closed surface Σ: [A]

∫∫_Σ K dA = 2πχ(Σ)

where χ is the Euler characteristic: - χ(S²) = 2 → total curvature = 4π - χ(T²) = 0 → total curvature = 0

The total curvature is a topological invariant. It cannot change by smooth deformation — only by changing genus. [A]

II. CURVATURE ON THE TORUS

2.1 The Distribution

For a torus with major radius R and minor radius r, parametrized by (θ, φ): [A]

K(θ) = cos(θ) / (r(R + r·cos(θ)))

This gives: - Outer equator (θ = 0): K > 0 (spherical curvature) - Top and bottom circles (θ = π/2, 3π/2): K = 0 (flat) - Inner equator (θ = π): K < 0 (saddle curvature)

2.2 The Curvature Cancellation

The positive curvature on the outer surface exactly cancels the negative curvature on the inner surface. Total = 0. This is not accidental — it is forced by Gauss-Bonnet and χ(T²) = 0. [A]

2.3 Physical Consequence: Geodesic Behavior

On the outer equator (K > 0): two particles started on nearby geodesics will converge. They are drawn together.

On the inner equator (K < 0): two particles started on nearby geodesics will diverge. They are pushed apart.

On the flat circles (K = 0): two particles on nearby geodesics remain parallel. Neither attracted nor repelled.

The torus contains all three behaviors simultaneously. Convergence, divergence, and neutrality coexist on the same surface. [A]

III. CURVATURE ON THE SPHERE

3.1 The Distribution

For S² with radius R: [A]

K = 1/R² everywhere

Constant. Positive. No exceptions. No regions of negative curvature. No regions of zero curvature. Every point on the sphere has the same curvature.

3.2 Geodesic Consequence

On S², every pair of geodesics (great circles) intersects. [A]

This is absolute. There are no parallel geodesics on a sphere. No two great circles can avoid each other. Every trajectory, no matter how it begins, eventually crosses every other trajectory.

The maximum separation between two geodesics occurs at a quarter-sphere distance — then they reconverge. The convergence is inescapable.

VII. CONSEQUENCES FOR THE LEVEL ARCHITECTURE

7.1 Curvature and L-Level

At different latitudes on S², while K is constant, the effective curvature radius of small circles varies. Near the poles, small circles have small radius — tight curvature. Near the equator, small circles have maximum radius — gentlest curvature. [A]

Level interpretation: At extreme L-levels (L1, L7), the convergence is tight — small perturbations snap back quickly. At L4 (equator), the convergence is gentle — the system has maximum room to explore before geodesics bring it back. [I]

L4 is the latitude of maximum geodesic freedom within universal convergence. Not freedom FROM convergence — freedom WITHIN it. [I]

Among all closed orientable surfaces embeddable in ℝ³, only S² has constant positive curvature. [A — Liebmann's theorem]

The torus cannot have constant positive curvature (Gauss-Bonnet forces total to 0). Higher-genus surfaces have even more negative curvature (total = 2π(2 − 2g), increasingly negative).

If constant positive curvature is the geometric requirement for "all geodesics reconverge" in this closed-surface model, then S² is the unique closed orientable surface that achieves it. There is exactly one topology for the framework's systemic-awareness model under those assumptions. The uniqueness of S² is theorem-level; identifying it with systemic awareness is interpretive.
