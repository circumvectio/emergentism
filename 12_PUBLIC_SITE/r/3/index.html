01
The Three Moves

Peirce's three inferential moves

Induction moves from particulars to a pattern: I observe N instances of a regularity, and I propose the regularity as a hypothesis. Induction is the first power: it generates the candidate claim from the data.

Deduction moves from a hypothesis to consequences: if the hypothesis is true, what must follow? Deduction is the second power: it tests the candidate claim for internal consistency and predicts observable consequences.

Abduction moves from a consequence back to a plausible hypothesis: an unexpected observation suggests a hypothesis that, if true, would explain it. Abduction is the third power: it generates the new claim that survives induction and deduction. Abduction is also the only move that can produce a new idea.

02
The Mapping

The varna mapping

The three powers map onto the L2, L3, L4 varna:

  • L2 (Śūdra) = Induction. Kālī is the disclosure. She surfaces the candidate patterns from the data. The first power: grammar → particulars → pattern.
  • L3 (Vaiśya) = Deduction. Kṛṣṇa is the audit. He bottlenecks the consequences. The second power: hypothesis → consequence → testable predictions.
  • L4 (Kṣatriya) = Abduction. Arjuna is the commit. He generates the new claim under uncertainty and signs with K2. The third power: consequence → plausible hypothesis → irreversible act.

The L4 equator (φ = ν = 1) is the abduction frontier: where viability and coherence balance, the only place where a new claim can be committed with K2.

03
The Order

The cycle's operational order

Peirce's research order is Abduction → Deduction → Induction: start with a surprising observation, propose a hypothesis, test it, refine it. The Emergentism cycle's operational order is the reverse: Induction → Deduction → Abduction.

The cycle enters at induction (L2 disclosure of particulars) and exits at abduction (L4 commitment). Deduction is the bottleneck in between. The two orders are not contradictory; they describe the same three moves from two angles. The research order is the discovery order; the operational order is the commitment order. A new claim that survives the cycle is one that has been induced, deductively tested, and abductively committed.

04
The 4th Power

Analogy and its disposition

Peirce later added a fourth inferential move: analogy (or hypothesis in some readings), which moves from proportion to proportion: a:b :: c:d, what is d? Analogy is the move that produces metaphor and cross-domain transfer: it is the move behind most of the framework's [I] interpretive claims.

The framework's disposition on the 4th power is to fold it into abduction: when analogy produces a new cross-domain claim, that claim is treated as an abductive candidate and routed through induction + deduction before commitment. This is the framework's way of honoring Peirce's 4th power while keeping the cycle's operational grammar at three moves.

[C] The claim that the three powers (or three+1) are exhaustive is itself a [C] claim. Some readings include further powers (e.g. retroduction in some Peircean taxonomies). The framework's three-power operational grammar is a pragmatic compression, not a final philosophical claim.
