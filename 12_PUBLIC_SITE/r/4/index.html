The Seven Questions

Each L-level of the framework answers a single key question. The seven questions form a ladder in operational order: the first question (L1) sets the end; the next three (L2, L3, L4) carry the operative cycle; the last three (L5, L6, L7) witness the whole. The questions are in process order, not in worth order.

01
The Ladder

The seven questions, in order

The key-question ladder is one of the Rosetta's most legible columns: each L-level carries a single question that names its functional scope. The questions are not rhetorical; they are operational specs. A claim at L3 is a claim that answers "What follows necessarily?" — which means the claim is about deductive inference, not about value or about Being.

The ladder is also a diagnostic: when a claim is in dispute, the first question to ask is "which level does this claim belong to?" If the answer is unclear, the claim is probably misplaced. A claim about the meaning of life belongs to L7; a claim about the structure of S² belongs to L5; a claim about the L3 → L4 bridge belongs to L3-L4 (and the bridge is L4's, not L3's).

02
The Entry-Point

Why L1 is the entry-point

L1 ("What is the end?") is the first question, not the most important. Without an answer to L1, the rest of the ladder has no input. L1 names the telos, the objective function, the wide-and-long light cone. The framework's L1 reading is the F5/Will to Potential: the drive to widen and lengthen the forward light cone, more space reachable and more time endurable.

L1 is not a value (L4 is value). L1 is not a should; it is a direction. The L1 entry-point does not tell you what to do; it tells you what direction the system is reaching. The decision of what to do about that direction is L4's, not L1's.

03
The Distinction

Operational order vs worth order

The seven questions are in operational order: L1 is the entry, L2-L4 carry the cycle, L5-L7 are the witness. They are not in worth order: L1 is not "lower than" L7. The operational order is a process order; the worth order would be a status order.

Conflating the two is the most common public misreading of the framework. The misreading is: "L7 is the highest level, so L1 is the lowest." This is wrong. The framework's η = 0 fence explicitly forbids this reading. L1 is the first level, not the lowest; L7 is the last level, not the highest. First and last are temporal/process markers, not worth markers.

04
The Disambiguation

How to map a question to its level

A question belongs to a level if the level's operator can act on it. A question that Kālī (L2) can act on is an L2 question: it can be disclosed, evidenced, surfaced. A question that Kṛṣṇa (L3) can act on is an L3 question: it can be ranked, audited, bottlenecked. A question that Arjuna (L4) can act on is an L4 question: it can be committed with K2.

A question that no operator can act on is either: (a) premature (the framework doesn't yet have the receipts to answer it), or (b) malplaced (the question is being asked at the wrong level). The framework's disposition is to defer (a) and re-locate (b).

[C] The claim that the seven questions are exhaustive — that they cover all the questions worth asking — is itself a [C] claim. Some readings include a L0 question (the unbounded, the meta-question that frames all the others) or fold L5-L7 into a single boundary. We present the seven here as the canonical Rosetta reading; alternative readings are not excluded.
