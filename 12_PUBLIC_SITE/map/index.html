How to read this

Observation first, geometry second.

The first claim is empirical-comparative: seven rows repeatedly align across several domains. The operators are then inferred as the simplest row-generators that preserve the pattern. The sphere is inferred after the rows and operators, not assumed at the start.

Evidence weights

The table is tiered, not flattened.

[A] is the proven math (the sphere identity, AM-GM). [S] is structural. [I] is interpretive — where the cross-domain Rosetta mappings live. [C] is conjectural. The caste/operator alignments are [I]: suggestive structure, never the evidential load — promoting them would trip this page's own kill-criterion.

Kill criteria

The Rosetta must be allowed to weaken.

If the seven-row alignment is shown to be forced, or if new strong domains break the alignment rather than refine it, the Rosetta weakens or falls. A public canon has to preserve that failure mode.

Scope guard

The Rosetta maps beings and practices, not the bedrock.

The Rosetta is strongest as a cross-domain map of beings, practices, initiations, operators, and disclosed structures. It does not directly prove the core state. The ontological reading remains tiered and must be argued separately.

Kill criterion for the Rosetta

  • If the seven-row alignment is shown to be forced by selection bias (only traditions that fit were included), the Rosetta weakens. [C]
  • If a major new domain (e.g., a robust 8-level or 6-level taxonomy from an independent tradition) breaks rather than refines the alignment, the Rosetta falls. [C]
  • If the mathematical derivation (φ·ν = 1, AM-GM optimum) is shown to contain an error, the formal spine collapses and the Rosetta becomes a curious table, not a map. [A]
  • If any column is promoted from [I] to [B] without independent evidence, the compass has been tampered with. [S]
