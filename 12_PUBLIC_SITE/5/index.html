01
The Geometry

Burrisphere as S² / ℂP¹ Public Geometry

The Burrisphere is the Riemann sphere S² reinterpreted as a constraint surface for coupled variables. Where the ordinary Riemann sphere maps complex numbers to points on a sphere, the Burrisphere maps coherence (φ) and viability (ν) to reciprocal coordinates that multiply to one everywhere on the open surface.

The equation φ · ν = 1 is not decorative. It is a binding constraint. At every point on the sphere except the poles, coherence and viability are locked in an inverse relationship. Raise one, and the other must fall. The only point where both can be equal is the equator: φ = ν = 1.

This is the geometric origin of the Power-Max Lemma: the equator is the unique point where balanced power is maximized. Not the north pole (pure coherence, zero viability). Not the south pole (pure viability, zero coherence). The equator, where both are unity and balance is perfect.

02
The Projections

Dual Stereographic Projections

The Burrisphere carries two stereographic projections, one from each pole — one cast downward from the north pole (∞), reading the sphere in the coherence coordinate φ = cot(θ/2); one cast upward from the south pole (0), reading it in the viability coordinate ν = tan(θ/2). Both rays pass through the same point of the sphere — they meet on the surface — and land on the equatorial plane at reciprocal radii. That is the equation: φ · ν = 1.

This duality is the geometric engine of the framework. Every decision, every system, every organism can be read as a point on the sphere — a particular trade-off between coherence and viability. The north-pole projection asks: "given this coherence, what viability is possible?" The south-pole projection asks: "given this viability, what coherence is possible?"

The two projections meet at the equator, where both questions have the same answer: φ = ν = 1. The equator is the only point where the two projections agree — where coherence and viability see each other clearly.

03
Game Space

Why Balance Is a Conditional Equilibrium

In game-space language, the Burrisphere is not just a manifold — it is a game board. The two stereographic projections trade viability (ν) and coherence (φ). Every agent on the sphere is playing the same game: maximise balance, maintain equilibrium, navigate toward the equator.

The extraction coefficient η measures asymmetry of exchange — how much a system takes relative to what it gives. η = 0 names balanced exchange: neither side gains at the other's expense. But balanced exchange is not an unconditional Nash equilibrium. Left to private incentives a player can still do better by defecting; and no mechanism can be strategyproof, efficient, and budget-balanced at once (Green–Laffont, 1977) [A]. So η = 0 is a conditional equilibrium — it holds only when enforcement (reputation, reciprocity, a licensed boundary) makes defection unprofitable [S/I]. The equator is where the game can rest, not where it rests on its own.

The poles are the imbalanced extremes: structure with no capacity to act at one, capacity with no coherence at the other (B → 0 at both). Neither pole is "extraction" — extraction is a direction of transfer (taking from a cooperator, η > 0), not a place on the sphere [S]. And an extractive move is self-defeating: to take from another you must first become a taker. The sustainable position is the equator — reached and held only under the constitution's refusals, not because the geometry commands it [S/I].

04
The Mirror

The Equator as the Playable Mirror

The equator is not merely a mathematical curiosity. It is the playable mirror — the position from which a system can act without being consumed by its own structure or dissolved by its own freedom. At the equator, the organism has enough coherence to see options and enough viability to actualize one.

The seven positions of the Rosetta are arranged around this sphere. Each position is a different latitude — a different balance of coherence and viability. The poles themselves are L0 and L∞ (B → 0); L1 and L7 sit nearest them. L4 sits at the equator (B = 1). The three Gods (L2–L4) rise through the southern hemisphere to the equator; the three Executive Boundaries (L5–L7) stand in the northern hemisphere above it.

The framework's entire taxonomy — castes, operators, disciplines, questions — is derived from this geometry. Change the geometry, and the framework changes. Deny the geometry, and the framework becomes arbitrary. The Burrisphere is not an illustration of the doctrine. It is the doctrine.
