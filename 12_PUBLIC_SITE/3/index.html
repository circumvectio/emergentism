§2 · The Dissolutions

The Sorites, Cured by the Invariance Gradient

The paradox. A body swaps every atom over years (Ship of Theseus; the sorites heap). Remove one plank, one cell, one grain — still the same body? Then when did it stop being itself? The classical trap demands a sharp threshold where identity flips, and finds none.

The cure named: invariance-gradient reality. [I] The paradox assumes identity is a sharp predicate over parts. D3 replaces it: a body's identity is the invariant it conserves across the gradient of its parts — its form, its metabolic closure, its history-with-continuity — not the parts themselves. There is no threshold because identity was never in the substrate; it is the pattern held stable across constrained degrees of freedom (the DoF reading of form, W1 [C], marked as wager). The heap dissolves because "heap" names a gradient, not a knife-edge.

What it does not claim. This is a dissolution by re-framing [I], not a proof that identity is metaphysically sharp — and it carries its debt: why this invariant persists (metabolic self-maintenance) is asserted at the reading tier, not derived. It does not dissolve the Hard Problem, does not make persistence a measured quantity, and does not license "any gradual thing is the same thing." It relocates the question from which part to which invariant, and hands the invariant back to biology to measure.

Foundations · biology is not geometry by decree

Principles and assumptions for bodies

Biology has no single universally accepted formal axiom set. D3 therefore distinguishes established scientific principles, domain-limited heuristics, and the assumptions of its own node model.

Modern cell theory · inherited science

The cellular foundation

  • Cellular organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  • The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of cellular life.
  • New cells arise from pre-existing cells.

These are established biological generalizations, not axioms from which every fact about life is deduced.

Thermodynamics + evolution · inherited science

Constraints every body still pays

Biological processes conserve energy, and an open organism can maintain local order only while exchanging energy and entropy with its environment. Evolution by natural selection requires heritable variation and differential reproductive success. Neither principle is ours, and neither alone defines life or identity.

Liebig · inherited heuristic

The limiting-factor rule

Liebig's law of the minimum says growth can be constrained by the scarcest essential resource. It motivates a need-both model when essential, non-substitutable factors are empirically demonstrated. It is an agronomic principle, not a universal axiom of every organism.
