The only lawful move lifts both players.

Ethics restated as a game rule, not a sermon: a move counts only where you and the whole preserve or raise one another. The move that grows one side by draining the other has a name — extraction — and, at scale, it eats its own board.

The discovery

One rule, two directions at once.

Most moral systems hand you a list. This one hands you a gate. Before any move — a trade, a promise, a line of code, a business model — ask one question in both directions: after this move, are you better off, and is the whole you belong to better off? Not "on average," not "eventually, trust us." Both sides, this move, with the burden of proof on the mover.

Four outcomes are possible, and only one is legal play. You rise and the whole rises: lawful — play it. Both fall: folly — nobody wanted this. You sacrifice yourself to lift the whole: noble sometimes, but not sustainable as a rule, because it burns out the player. And the fourth — you rise by making the whole fall — is the interesting one. That is extraction, and the framework's entire ethics compresses into refusing it: the extraction dial is set to zero.

Why zero, and not "keep extraction small"? Because extraction compounds. Every extractive move slightly degrades the board — the trust, the soil, the counterparty — that all future moves are played on. Run it long enough at scale and the game runs out of board: the strategy self-terminates, and it takes the table with it. That is not a moral intuition dressed up as an argument; it is the structural claim, and it is a conditional one — it holds where the players stay inside one shared game long enough for the degradation to come home. We say so, because our own auditors made us say so.

Notice what the rule does not demand. It does not demand altruism, purity, or agreement about the good. It demands only that you not be the move that eats the board. Everything above that floor — generosity, sacrifice, ambition — stays yours to play.

Not "be good." Just this: never grow by draining what sustains you.

Why this matters to you

It gives you a one-question audit that works on anything with two sides: a job offer, a pricing model, a relationship, an algorithm. Who rises, who falls, this move? The gate is fast, it is symmetric, and it is very hard to gaslight — because it never asks about intentions, only about the two arrows. Most of what feels vaguely wrong in modern life fails this test in under a minute; most of what quietly works, passes it.
