One point, two shadows.

Every being casts a seeing-shadow and a doing-shadow — and the geometry says the summit is the middle.

The discovery

Every being is one point casting two shadows.

Call them seeing and doing. The Greek letters are φ (phi, seeing: how truly you model the world) and ν (nu, doing: how effectively you move in it). You know people who are almost all one shadow — the brilliant observer who never acts, the tireless operator who never looks. The model puts both on a sphere.

Picture a point on a globe at some latitude θ. Draw the two chords from that point to the poles. Elementary geometry — nothing fancier than a right angle in a semicircle — gives you a balance number: B = sin θ. It can never exceed 1, and it peaks at the equator. Not at either pole. The summit of balance is the exact middle.

Two things must not be confused here, and we say so on every page where they appear:

The seam

φ·ν = 1 — identically

In the chart we use, this holds by construction — bookkeeping, like a fraction times its reciprocal. It certifies the coordinates, and it proves nothing about you.

The score

B = sin θ ≤ 1

The measurement. It varies from person to person, day to day, and the equator is its maximum.

The seam is the frame; the score is the picture. Confusing them is the oldest error in this business, and our own auditors caught us leaning near it — which is why the distinction is now printed in bold.

Why this matters to you

It kills the purity fantasy — in both directions.

Total vision with no action scores zero. Total action with no vision scores zero. And — this is the part that stings — moving past the equator toward either pole lowers your score. More seeing is not always better. More doing is not always better. The geometry has an optimum, and the optimum is balance, and balance is not a compromise. It is the peak.

One honest reading of your own latitude, this week: which shadow is longer?

Held honestly

The geometry itself is settled — it is chords on a circle, and you can check it with a pencil. That your life maps onto a latitude is our wager, not our theorem.
