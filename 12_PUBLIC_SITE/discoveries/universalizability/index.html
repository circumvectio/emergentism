The whole ethic is one asymmetric bit.

Run any way of living through one test: what if everyone did this? Cooperation survives its own universalization. Extraction does not — a world of pure takers has no one left to take from. The test is Kant's, credited in full. The discovery is where our geometry lands when you push it: on exactly that bit.

The discovery

Symbiosis scales. Demonhood cannot.

Take the extractor's strategy seriously — call it demonhood: grow yourself by draining others, always. As a private strategy it can work brilliantly; history is full of successful parasites. Now universalize it. A world where everyone extracts is a world where every move needs a victim and no one is left to be one. The strategy is a logical free-rider: it only works while most people refuse it. It cannot even be stated as a law for everyone without deleting its own precondition.

Now universalize the opposite strategy — grow only where both sides rise. A world where everyone plays that way does not collapse. It compounds. That is the asymmetry, and it is the entire result: one strategy passes the everyone-test, its mirror image fails it. One bit. The parasite needs the host; the host never needed the parasite.

Credit where it is due, in bold: the test is Immanuel Kant's — the categorical imperative, 1785, not ours. We derived no new moral logic. What we found is that our geometry of players and wholes, pushed to the question "which strategies can be everyone's strategy?", lands precisely on Kant's answer, with the extraction bit doing all the work. As with the physics our seam inherits, the rhyme is the discovery, not the theft — the same asymmetry showing up from an independent direction is evidence the pattern is real, not evidence that we invented it.

And one more honesty, the most important on this page. The test tells you which strategies can be universal. It does not, by itself, force you to care. No amount of is ever fully purchases an ought — Hume's gap stays open, and we do not pretend to close it. What the framework does is compress everything the gap cannot cover into this single asymmetric bit, and then take it as a vow: refuse the non-universalizable move. A vow is not a theorem. We hold it as a vow, and we say so.

Everything is/ought cannot bridge, compressed to one bit — and the bit is taken as a vow.

Why this matters to you

It replaces a thousand-page ethics with a ten-second test you can run on anything: a habit, a hustle, a policy, a prompt. Ask "what if everyone did this?" — and watch whether the strategy needs other people to be doing something else. If it does, it is a parasite on the very norm it violates, however clever it looks up close. You do not need to believe anything on this site to use that test. It was Kant's before it was ours, and it will work for you whether or not you ever read another page here.
