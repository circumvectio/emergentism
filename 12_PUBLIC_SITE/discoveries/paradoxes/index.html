One key,
twenty-six locks.

Zeno's arrow. The liar. The ship of Theseus. Free will against determinism. The gap between is and ought. Philosophy's most famous knots have resisted for millennia — and most of them turn out to be one sentence doing two jobs at once. Separate the jobs, and the knot falls open in your hands.

The discovery

A paradox is a register collision.

Here is the move, once, so you can use it yourself. Every description of the world runs in a register — a level of bookkeeping. There is the seam register, where things are true by construction, the way a fraction times its reciprocal is one. There is the score register, where things vary and can be measured. And there is the node register, where a whole thing either lives or dies. We keep these three rigorously apart everywhere on this site — because confusing them is not just our occupational hazard. It is philosophy's oldest occupational hazard.

A paradox is what it feels like when one sentence silently straddles two registers. "This sentence is false" asks a statement to be its own scorekeeper — the claim and the audit of the claim, fused into one clause. Zeno slices continuous motion into an infinite ledger of completed steps, then acts surprised that the ledger never closes: he billed a process in the currency of finished things. The ship of Theseus asks whether the ship is "the same" without saying same in which register — same planks (no), same self-maintaining pattern (yes). The question was never hard. It was underspecified.

One key, tried on twenty-six locks. Not all of them open the same way, and we say which is which — every dissolution in the suite carries its own tier and its own way to be wrong. A sample:

01Zeno's arrow

Motion billed as an infinite ledger of finished steps. Process and inventory are different registers. The arrow flies; the ledger was the confusion.

02The liar

A claim fused with its own audit. Separate statement from scorekeeping and the sentence isn't false — it's doing two jobs no sentence can hold at once.

03The ship of Theseus

"Same" without a register is not a question. Same parts: no. Same self-maintaining pattern: yes. Both answers are true — of different registers.

04Free will vs. determinism

Physics describes the trajectory; agency describes the selection among modeled futures. Two registers of the same event — a collision, not a contradiction.

05Is and ought — handled differently

The one lock we do not claim to pick with logic. Hume's gap is real. We compress it to a single asymmetric bit — a vow, not a derivation: refuse extraction, because a world of extractors cannot exist. The demon's position doesn't universalize; ours is chosen, openly, as a wager.

26…and twenty-one more

The full suite — Fermi, the measurement problem, the hard problem, the problem of evil, more — is public, each with its tier and its kill-criterion, in the paradox library.

If a 2,400-year-old knot opens in a paragraph, suspect the key — then check whether it turns.

Why this matters to you: the register move is not for antiquity's puzzles. It is for Tuesday. The argument you keep having that never resolves — with a partner, a co-founder, yourself — is very often two registers wearing one word. "Is this working?" (score) versus "is this alive?" (node) versus "is this what we said it was?" (seam). Split the sentence into its jobs before you fight about the answer. Most fights are collisions.

And the honest frame: dissolving a paradox is not the same as solving it, and a lens that opens many locks might be a skeleton key or might be a hammer that makes everything look like a nail. That is a real risk, we name it, and it is exactly why every dissolution is graded separately and kept on trial rather than declared. The readings are ours; the humility is enforced.
