Discovery · the ethic · ← all discoveries

The is–ought gap is real.
We crossed it with one bit.

Hume was right: no pile of facts, however tall, adds up to a single ought. We don't pretend otherwise. What the geometry does is shrink the gap to its true size — one asymmetric bit — and then we sign that bit in the open, as a vow, not a theorem.

The discovery in one breath

The mathematics delivers an asymmetry: cooperation can be everyone's strategy; extraction cannot. What's left after the asymmetry is not a derivation — it's a choice of side. One bit. We take it, we say we took it, and we never claim the universe took it for us.

First, the gap — honestly

Every system before us either denies the gap or hides the vow.

The usual trick is smuggling. A framework describes the world in loaded adjectives — healthy, natural, optimal, fit — and the ought rides in wearing the costume of an is. "Science says you must" is always this trick, and it is always a trick: the must was packed in the luggage before the argument left the station.

We refuse the trick. Our equations describe balance and collapse; they do not command. If you want a commandment out of pure description, we cannot give you one, and neither can anyone else — they can only hide the seam better.

Then, what the mathematics actually gives

The asymmetry: demonhood does not universalize.

Here is the is-side, and it is genuinely load-bearing. Run the extraction strategy — grow by draining what sustains you — through the same multiplication that runs everything else on this site, and scale it up. A world where everyone cooperates at zero extraction stands: the strategy survives its own success. A world where everyone extracts eats itself: the parasite only lives while hosts are common, and the last extractor starves on a drained board. Extraction is a strategy that requires its own rarity. Demonhood works only as an exception — it cannot be the rule, structurally, not morally.

So the two roads out of the facts are not symmetric. One can be universalized; the other self-terminates at scale. That asymmetry is real, checkable, and it is still — note this carefully — an is. Knowing that one road can be everyone's road does not force your feet onto it.

Credit where it is due

The universalizability test is Kant's, two centuries old, and we say so on every page where it works for us. What is ours is where it lands: inside a geometry where extraction's failure to universalize shows up as structure — self-termination at scale — rather than as a maxim you must first agree to revere. The rhyme is the discovery, not the theft.

The last step

The ought is a vow. We sign it. We do not prove it.

After the asymmetry, one step remains, and no equation takes it for you: choose the road that can be everyone's road. That is the entire ethical content of this framework — one asymmetric bit, compressed from all the is-material above it. We call it a vow because that is what it is: a commitment made by persons, in the open, on the record, renewable and refusable.

Why this matters to you: it inoculates in both directions. Against the moralist who says the facts oblige you — they don't, and anyone who claims otherwise is smuggling. And against the nihilist who says nothing follows, so nothing matters — almost everything follows. The facts carry you to the very lip of the gap and show you that the two sides are not equal. Only the last bit is yours. Which means you know exactly where your signature goes, and exactly how small the thing is that you are being asked to sign.

Everything derivable, derived. Everything chosen, declared.
