The μ-limit · the door between rungs

How one freedom opens the next

A degree of freedom, run to its limit, becomes the opening of the next. But there is a plainer way to say what the μ-limit does, and it is the thing to hold onto:

The μ-limit is the operation that makes an infinite object visible in full. A function that escapes off the top of the screen and returns from the bottom. A line that runs past both edges forever. These look broken — and they are not. The tear is in the chart. Close the chart and the same object is whole, finite, and entirely on screen.

One consequence is worth stating before anything moves. A function like 1/x "blows up" at zero only if you keep two points at infinity, so that −∞ and +∞ are different places. Glue them into one point and 1/x becomes a single continuous closed curve. Nothing about the function changed. Which infinity you choose is a choice, and only one of the two choices lets you see the thing entire. [A]

Below: the hinge, the line that closes, the pattern that repeats — and then the gallery, one example after another, of infinity brought onto the screen.

2 · The line that closes — 1D saturating into 2D

κ : 0 → 2π/L    radius r = 1/κ    (a line is a circle of infinite radius)
curvature κ 0.000radius r = 1/κ ∞ wrap κL/2π 0.00straight — 1D

Not slope — curvature. A straight line has κ = 0 and radius ∞; it is already a circle, one of infinite size. Raise κ and the same length of line bends; when κ·L = 2π the two ends meet and the circle is seen whole. The curve stays intrinsically one-dimensional the entire time — but the moment it closes, you need a second dimension to see it at all. [A]

3 · The pattern — and it repeats at every rung

Adding one point at infinity to an n-dimensional freedom closes it into the n-sphere — and the closed object can only be seen whole from n+1. That is standard topology (one-point compactification), and it is the μ-limit's shape, rung after rung:

4 · The window that cannot tell

sagitta  s = a² / (R + √(R²−a²))   →   undetectable when s < δ
radius R — windows bulge s — precision δ 0.004 indistinguishable from straight
all this window can honestly report: R > 31.3 windows bound check R(s) − R = 0.000000

The gold arc and the dashed chord are drawn at true aspect — no vertical exaggeration. At large R they are the same pixels, because the bulge really is smaller than the line is thick. The gauge measures that bulge against a stated precision δ. Cross the threshold and curvature becomes detectable; above it, the window is silent — and silence is not evidence of straightness. All a bounded window can ever return is a lower bound on R, never the verdict "infinite." [A]

And the distinction it is straining to detect may not even be intrinsic. On the sphere, lines and circles are a single family: a line is exactly a circle through the point at infinity, and a Möbius map carries any circle to a line and back. So "straight" is not a property of the curve — it is a statement about where infinity was placed. The line was not approximately a circle. It was one. [A] used, not discovered

This is not a toy. It is how cosmology treats the shape of space: measurements constrain the curvature to be small, which places a lower bound on the radius of curvature — if space is closed, its radius exceeds the region we can see. No measurement returns "flat and infinite"; the observable universe is the window, and the window cannot tell. [A] the method · [I] the reading

The fence, and it cuts both ways. None of this shows the line is closed. It shows you cannot tell from here — which forbids the confident "it goes on forever" exactly as much as it forbids the confident "it comes back." The honest output of a bounded window is a bound, not a belief. Treating unboundedness as the default is a choice too, and it is the one that usually goes unexamined. [S]

5 · The pole — up off the top, back from the bottom

y = 1/x  ·  v = (2/π) arctan y  ·  glue v = +1 to v = −1  ⇒  ℝ ∪ {∞}
x 0.000 y = 1/x 0.000 x·y 1.000000 |+∞ − −∞| 0.000 px chart two-point

The vertical axis is compactified, the horizontal one is not: v = (2/π) arctan y sends +∞ and −∞ to the two edges, and the axis then rolls until those edges are one location — the roll is presentational; every intermediate frame is still two-point, and the identification happens only in the last instant, when the ends coincide. Read as two points, the branch ends stay apart and 1/x has no continuous extension at x = 0; read as one point, the ends coincide, the separation readout falls to 0.000 px, and the travelling dot crosses ∞ without stopping. The y readout shows the finite float the parametrisation actually produces; it never literally prints ∞, because the drawing never divides by zero. The tear was in the chart, not in the function — which infinity you take is a choice, and only the one-point choice makes the object whole. One-point compactification is inherited topology, used here, not discovered; the dot still leaves the frame at |x| > 3, because the x-axis was deliberately left open. [A] [S]

6 · The projective line — one-point compactification

x = tan(θ/2)  ·  θ = 2 arctan x  ·  sin θ = 2x/(1+x²), cos θ = (1−x²)/(1+x²)  ·  ℝ ∪ {∞} ≅ S¹  ·  one lap = 2π
x 0.000θ 0.0000 rad|P(x) − P(θ)| 0.00e+0state near 0

Every line through N except the horizontal tangent at N meets the number line exactly once, and meets the circle exactly once more besides N itself. That one exceptional direction — the only one with no partner on the line — is what the extra point ∞ names. So all of ℝ, plus one point, fits inside a finite circle. +∞ and −∞ are the same place here by choice of compactification: this is ℝ ∪ {∞}, one point added, not the two-endpoint line [−∞, +∞] of elementary calculus, where they stay distinct. The moving point travels at constant speed on the circle while its chart coordinate x = tan(θ/2) accelerates, leaves the frame past the last tick (the thickened arc at the top is everything beyond the visible line), passes through N, and returns from the far left: the blow-up is in the number, not in the point. The readout builds the same circle point down two chains that share only θ — from θ by sine and cosine, and from x = tan(θ/2) by actually intersecting the ray N→(x,0) with the circle — and prints their difference. That tests the half-angle identities and the intersection algebra; it does not test θ itself. This is the one-dimensional case of the move that turns the plane into the sphere. The whole plane is here, not a window onto it: stereographic projection wraps every point of it onto the sphere, so the entire thing fits inside one finite outline — the silhouette of that sphere, seen straight on, so near and far halves of the surface overlap in the drawing. Each straight grid line arrives as a circle, and all of those circles meet at the top point, which is the image of infinity — an ordinary point of the surface, with nothing singular about it, though it is the one point that every straight line passes through. The plane rotates and zooms out tenfold every thirteen seconds, so arbitrarily distant regions sweep in through that point while the image radius stays exactly the sphere radius; readout (c) builds the cyan circle twice down independent chains — three mapped points versus the closed form — and shows the difference vanish, while readout (d) confirms that the circle fitted to three ordinary finite points passes through the image of infinity to machine precision. This is the one-point compactification: every direction of escape arrives at the same single ∞, unlike the projective closure where opposite directions land on distinct points of a circle at infinity. Stereographic projection is inherited mathematics, used, not discovered. [A] [S]

10 · The periodic pole — tan, and why it repeats

tan θ = height where the line O P meets x = 1  ·  tan(θ + π) − tan θ = 0  (θ ≠ π/2 + kπ)
θ 0.0000tan θ 0.0000circle angle 0.00°|OP| 1.000000000000tan θ − tan(θ+π) 0 (exact)

Left is the graph of tan; right is the same θ on the unit circle, where tan θ is read off as the height at which the line through the centre cuts the tangent line x = 1. At θ = π/2 that line is parallel to x = 1, so the height has nowhere to land — meanwhile the point crosses the top of the circle at the same steady speed it has everywhere, and |OP| stays 1 to twelve decimals. The height depends only on the line, not on which of its two ends carries the point, so the antipodal chain returns the identical number: the displayed difference tan θ − tan(θ+π) is exactly 0 — exact by construction, not by numerical luck: the two chains are the same line read from its two ends; π is a period, and the graph beside it shows the branch does not repeat any sooner. The trigonometry here is [A] used, not discovered — including the reading of tan θ as the intercept on the tangent line x = 1, which is the classical construction the function is named after. Nothing on this canvas is framework-derived.

12 · An infinite hyperbolic plane in a finite disk — and how it differs

r = tanh(s / 2)  ·  d(0,P) = ln [(1+r) / (1−r)] = 2 artanh r  ·  s → ∞ : r → 1, d → ∞
hyperbolic distance from centre 0.000Euclidean picture radius 0.000000000largest residual, three closures 0.0e+0geodesic angle at the rim 90.000°total distance travelled 0.0

The cyan point moves at constant hyperbolic speed — one gold ring, one unit of distance, one second — so its picture radius climbs toward 1 while the distance readout climbs at a steady one unit per second. The run is cut at distance 12, where the point is already within 0.003 of a pixel of the rim: the picture and the arithmetic both run out long before the distance does, and past that point only the number would still be telling the truth. Distance is computed forward as r = tanh(s / 2) and then read back out of the picture as the log cross-ratio of four Euclidean distances to the two ideal endpoints of the point's own geodesic — a round trip, not two independent derivations. What it certifies is that the point really moves at unit hyperbolic speed; it is not evidence for the metric itself, which is inherited mathematics here, used, not discovered. The largest residual — cross-ratio against arclength, Cayley against arclength, and the orthogonality identity |Q|² − ρ² = 1 for the highlighted geodesic — stays at rounding level: worst about 1e-12 at the far end, where |PA| = 1 − r is a subtraction of nearly equal numbers and the arithmetic, not the geometry, is what degrades. This is not a one-point compactification: the rim is an ideal boundary at infinite distance, it carries a whole circle of distinct ideal points rather than one added point. The closed disk you are looking at is a compactification — of the model, by its ideal boundary, which for the hyperbolic plane is canonically a circle and not an artefact of this chart — but the added set is a whole circle of points at infinite distance, and unlike the single added point of the one-point construction, none of it belongs to the space. That is why it is drawn dashed and why the point never lands on it. What this shares with the other instruments is only that an infinite object is shown whole in a finite frame; the crowding at the rim is a fact about the chart, not about the plane. [A] [S] [I]

The honest fence

The geometry is inherited and exact. The hinge map, constant-curvature closure, and one-point compactification (ℝⁿ ∪ {∞} ≅ Sⁿ) are standard mathematics — used, not discovered [A].

The reading is ours, and the causal claim is a wager. That these geometric closures are the ladder's rungs is an interpretation [I]. That measurable saturation of one register raises the probability of a genuinely new freedom emerging is μ-at-saturation, held at [C] with its kill named: exhibit a lower-rung theory that predicts the next rung's genesis and the wager dies.

Two registers kept apart. Closing a freedom (compactification) and gaining a dimension are different moves: the circle stays intrinsically 1D. What the closure forces is a viewing dimension. We do not collapse the two.
