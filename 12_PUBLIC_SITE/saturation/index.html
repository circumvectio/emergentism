The Hinge

A degree of freedom, run to its limit, becomes the opening of the next. That saturation has a function — and it is nothing exotic: the odds function, which takes the finite interval and unrolls it onto the whole ray.

L(x) = x / (1 − x)  :  [0, 1)  →  [0, ∞)
x (bounded) 0.000 L(x) = x/(1−x) 0.000 distance to the μ-limit  1 − x = 1.000

The bar above is the bounded freedom [0, 1). The bar below is the unbounded one [0, ∞). As x saturates toward the μ-limit at 1, its image runs to ∞ — the finite interval unrolls into the whole ray. The pole is the saturation. [A]

Why this is the door, not a room

The line, then the hinge, then the sphere. The reciprocal line (D1) carries one bounded coordinate. This hinge takes that coordinate to its limit and manufactures the one thing the line lacked — a point at infinity. And a ray with its point at infinity, closed up, is the sphere (D2). So the μ-limit is exactly the passage D1 → D2: line → (pole) → sphere.

Approached, never occupied. The point never reaches x = 1 — at the μ-limit the function has no value, only a limit. Saturation is a horizon a freedom runs toward, never a place it stands. (The same shape as the poles, and as the speed of light on the passage.)

The honest fence

The function is inherited. L(x)=x/(1−x) is the odds/logit map, standard mathematics — used, not discovered [A]. Reading its pole as "the μ-limit, where one freedom opens the next" is our interpretation [A] the pole · [I] the reading.

The strong claim stays a wager. That measurable saturation of one register actually raises the probability of a genuinely new freedom emerging is μ-at-saturation, held at [C] — a conjecture with a stated kill, not a theorem. This page shows the shape of the door; that emergence walks through it is the bet.

Kept in its own frame. This is the D1→D2 transition — distinct from the reciprocal line, the egg chart, the sphere, and the torus family. A hinge is not a room; we do not fuse it with the rungs it joins.
