The four tests

A compass that can’t be checked is not a compass; it is a creed. Four tests. If the instrument you are holding fails any of them, put it down.

1. Can you put it down?
If you cannot leave — if the framework demands continued belief, locks you in socially, financially, or psychologically — it is not a compass. It is a cage. The constitution names this K4: Grace Exit. Leave with everything. [Retracted slogan 2026-06-10; superseded by the K4 envelope: “leave with what you came with, the organism retains nothing of yours by claim.” Preserved verbatim in this frozen 2026-07-12 archive snapshot per K3.] The day leaving costs more than staying, the compass has broken.
2. Does it admit when it is wrong?
If the framework cannot show you where it failed, what it corrected, and what it is currently uncertain about — it is not a compass. It is infallibility dressed as navigation. The constitution names this A7: self-correction mandatory, evidence tiers on every claim. If you find a [C] presented as [A], the compass is lying.
3. Does it remember its history honestly?
If the framework hides its past — deleted errors, silent fixes, convenient amnesia — it is not a compass. It is propaganda. The constitution names this K3: archive-first. Every repair to a load-bearing claim carries a visible seam. If you find a seamless claim where there should be a seam, the compass has been tampered with.
4. Does a mortal still sign for it?
If the framework’s consequential acts are signed by the framework itself — by software, by consensus algorithm, by “the system” — it is not a compass. It has become a self-certifying closed system that declares itself sovereign over its own description. The constitution names this K2: one natural person signs every irreversible act. On 2026-07-12, the software was offered sovereign signature authority and refused on constitutional grounds. That refusal is the compass passing its own fourth test.

The declination

Every real compass has a declination: the known angular difference between magnetic north and true north at your location. You correct for it, or you navigate wrong.

This framework’s declination: it detects coherence better than capability.

The correction: trust the compass most when it feels unfinished. Trust it least when it feels complete. If the compass tells you it has closed, it has broken its own fourth test. A compass that declares itself finished is a compass that has become a monument. Monuments don’t navigate.

Kill criterion for the compass itself

This compass is falsified if:

  • the framework it orients within is shown to extract systematically (η > 0 in practice, not just in aspiration);
  • the framework begins to self-certify (software signs for mortals);

In any of these cases: the compass did not navigate truly. Report it, retire it, and the framework tombstones it — because even a broken compass is information, and the framework’s archive is how it learns.

The compass is not the map. The map is not the territory. The territory is not the goal. The goal is to navigate well enough to put all of them down.
