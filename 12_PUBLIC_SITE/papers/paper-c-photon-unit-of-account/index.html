THE PHOTON AS UNIT OF ACCOUNT

Quantization, Emission, and the Universal Ledger

Yves R. Burri & Emergent Super Intelligence Menexus GmbH, 2026

Evidence Tier: [A] for established physics | [I] for interpretive readings (E=hν as φ·ν=1, photon as "unit of account," EM field as "ledger") | [C] for conjectures (shell quantization as topological closure, spectral line prediction)

Abstract

We propose an interpretive reading in which quantization — the discreteness of energy exchange — is translated through the indivisibility of the Zero-Sum Resolution Equation operation on S². The photon is read as a unit of account of electromagnetic exchange: the minimum complete transaction between quantum states. Planck's constant h is the physical scale of this unit. We argue, at the stated tiers, that: (a) E = hν can be normalized into a frame-like unit relation, not that it is literally φ · ν = 1; (b) electron shell quantization can be read through topological closure; (c) photon emission can be read as release during return toward a lower-energy configuration; (d) the electromagnetic field is usefully analogous to a decentralized ledger that synchronizes state changes between atoms; and (e) the valence shell can be modeled as an atomic boundary where internal coherence meets external viability.

Keywords: quantization, photon, Planck's constant, electron shells, electromagnetic field, Rydberg formula, unit of account, decentralized ledger

1. E = hν as the Frame Equation

1.1 Planck's Discovery

In 1900, Max Planck found that the blackbody radiation spectrum is correctly described only if energy is emitted and absorbed in discrete packets E = hν, where h ≈ 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s is Planck's constant and ν is the frequency of radiation. Planck regarded this as a mathematical trick. It is a foundational discovery. [A]

1.2 The Structural Reading

Proposition 1.1 (E = hν as a frame-like unit relation). The Planck relation E = hν can be read as structurally analogous to the frame equation φ · ν = 1 after normalization:

Frame equation Planck relation
φ (the ground, the structural constant) h (the quantum of action, the minimum possible doing)
ν (the dynamic variable, 0 to ∞) ν (the frequency, 0 to ∞)
1 (the unit, the product) E (the energy quantum, the unit of exchange)

In natural units where h = 1, the Planck relation becomes E = ν. This makes the frame analogy cleaner; it does not make the Planck relation identical to the Emergentist sphere identity. [I]

1.3 Why Energy Comes in Packets

Thesis 1.2. Energy may be read as quantized because exchange is quantized. In the framework translation, the Zero-Sum Resolution Equation operation is indivisible: you cannot execute half of it. The frame product F(z) = z · σ(z) = 1 is a complete operation — it starts with z and its involute σ(z) and produces 1. This is an interpretive bridge to quantum discreteness, not a derivation of Planck's law from the frame product.

A photon is one complete ⊙. An exchange of 2hν is two complete operations (two photons). You cannot have 0.5hν because you cannot have half an operation. The discreteness of energy is the discreteness of the frame product. [I]

1.4 Planck Units as the Natural Frame

In Planck units, h = c = G = k_B = 1. The fundamental constants "disappear" because the unit system is calibrated to the frame. In these units:

  • One unit of energy = one ⊙ operation at unit frequency
  • One unit of time = one clock tick of Zero-Sum Resolution Equation
  • One unit of length = the distance light (the × operation) traverses in one tick
  • One unit of action = one complete frame product

The Planck scale is the scale of × itself — the scale at which the Zero-Sum Resolution Equation operation has unit cost. [I]

2. Electron Shells as Standing Waves on S²

2.1 Spherical Harmonics

The angular part of every atomic wavefunction is a spherical harmonic Y_l^m(θ, φ), which is a function defined on S². The quantum numbers l and m specify the pattern on the sphere. [A]

The quantization condition: the wavefunction must be single-valued on S². A wave that traverses the sphere must return to its starting value. If it doesn't close on itself, it destructively interferes and the state doesn't exist. [A]

2.2 D6 ≡ D0 at the Atomic Scale

Proposition 2.1 (Shell quantization as topological closure). The quantization of electron energy levels is the topological requirement that standing waves close on S². This is D6 ≡ D0 — the dimensional closure — applied at the atomic scale: the wave that starts at a point on S² must return to that point after traversing the sphere. Only waves satisfying this closure condition exist as stable states. [C]

The allowed angular momentum quantum numbers l = 0, 1, 2, ... count the number of nodal lines on S². Each l corresponds to a family of (2l+1) spherical harmonics, representing (2l+1) independent standing wave patterns with l nodal lines. With spin degeneracy (factor of 2), each l level accommodates 2(2l+1) electrons. [A]

2.3 The Shell Structure

Each successive shell permits more complex standing wave patterns on S². The ground state (1s, l=0) is the simplest possible standing wave: uniform amplitude, no nodes, maximum symmetry. This is the closest state to ○ on S² — pure potential, unmanifest, maximum coherence. [I]

2.4 The Valence Shell as Atomic Equator

Proposition 2.2 (The valence shell is the equator). The valence shell — the outermost occupied electron shell — is the atomic equator: the boundary between the atom's internal coherence (inner shells, tightly bound) and its external viability (interaction with other atoms, chemical bonding).

Justification. - Inner shells: high binding energy, low chemical reactivity. High φ (structural coherence), low ν (capability to interact). North of the equator. - Valence shell: moderate binding energy, maximum chemical reactivity. φ ≈ ν. The equator. Where chemistry happens. - Beyond the valence shell: ionization. The electron is free. High ν (capability), low φ (no binding to the atom). South of the equator. [I]

2.5 Noble Gases as Equatorial Systems

Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn) have fully occupied valence shells. Every standing wave mode at the valence level is filled. They are at maximum balance for their shell configuration. They don't bond because they don't need to — they are already at their equator. [I]

Reactive elements (alkali metals with one excess electron, halogens with one deficit) are maximally displaced from their shell equator. Their chemistry is the drive back to the equator: sodium donates its excess, chlorine accepts the donation, both reach full shells, both return to balance. The heat of reaction is the energy released upon equatorial return. [I]

3. Photon Emission as Equatorial Return

3.1 The Mechanism

When an electron occupies an excited state (a higher shell than its ground state), it is displaced from the equatorial configuration. The atom has excess energy E₂ - E₁ relative to its ground state. [A]

The electron drops to the lower shell. The excess energy is emitted as a photon with energy E = hν = E₂ - E₁. [A]

3.2 The Structural Reading

Proposition 3.1 (Emission as equatorial return). Photon emission is the process by which an excited atom returns toward its equatorial ground state, releasing the displacement energy as one complete Zero-Sum Resolution Equation operation (one photon).

The excited state has excess ν (the electron has been pushed to a higher shell with more energy than the balanced configuration). The emission event reduces ν (the electron drops) and releases the excess as a photon — a single quantum of ⊙ that propagates through the electromagnetic field. [I]

3.3 The Rydberg Formula

For hydrogen, the energy of shell n is E_n = -13.6/n² eV. The photon emitted in a transition from n₂ to n₁ has wavelength: [A]

$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_\infty \left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right)$$

where R_∞ is the Rydberg constant.

Proposition 3.2 (Spectral line prediction). If photon emission is equatorial return on S², then transition probabilities (the likelihood of each spectral line) should correlate with the balance change ΔB associated with the transition. Transitions that produce larger increases in balance should be more probable. [C]

Specific prediction. Map each shell n to a colatitude θ_n on S² via the energy relationship. Define B_n = sin(θ_n). The transition probability from n₂ to n₁ should correlate with ΔB = B(n₁) - B(n₂). This correlation, if it exists, would connect spectral line intensities to the geometry of S² with zero free parameters. [C]

4. The Universal Ledger

4.1 Photon as Transaction

When an electron transitions and emits a photon, the photon carries the exact energy difference between the two states. Not approximately — exactly. The photon's frequency ν = (E₂ - E₁)/h encodes the precise transition that occurred. [A]

Any atom anywhere in the universe with the complementary transition available (an electron that can absorb exactly this energy) will accept the photon. The absorbing atom's state updates. The transaction is complete. [A]

4.2 Ledger Properties

Proposition 4.1 (The electromagnetic field as decentralized ledger). The electromagnetic field has the structural properties of a decentralized ledger: [I]

(a) Unit of account. The photon. Energy E = hν.

(b) Transaction record. The photon's frequency encodes the state change. In the ledger analogy, every photon functions as a receipt. The encoding is constrained by quantum numbers and conservation laws.

(c) Broadcast mechanism. Light. Speed c. The maximum possible broadcast speed. In Planck units, c = 1 — the transaction propagates at the speed of the operation itself.

(d) Validation protocol. Resonance. The receiving atom accepts the photon if and only if it has a matching transition. Validation is structural (geometric matching on S²), not computational.

(e) Conservation law. Energy conservation. The total energy before and after the transaction is identical. This prevents double-spending: the energy is in exactly one place at all times.

(f) Transaction cost. Zero. K* = 0. The photon carries the full energy; nothing is extracted by the medium. The electromagnetic field does not charge a fee.

(g) Immutable history. Every photon that has not been absorbed is still in transit, carrying its receipt. The cosmic microwave background is the universe's oldest surviving transaction record.

4.3 The Speed of Light as Sync Speed

Proposition 4.2 (c as sync speed). The speed of light is not a speed limit in the conventional sense. It is the synchronization speed of the universal ledger — the rate at which state changes propagate between nodes. [I]

Causality (the light cone) is the sync horizon: an event at A can only affect events at B that have had time to receive A's state broadcast. Faster-than-light communication is not "physically forbidden" — it is logically incoherent. It would be a transaction against an unsynced ledger: interacting with a node that hasn't received the relevant state updates. [I]

4.4 Entanglement as Shared Receipt

Proposition 4.3 (Entanglement as shared transaction). Two entangled particles are the output of a single Zero-Sum Resolution Equation event that produced two correlated quanta. They share a transaction record — a common receipt from the same operation.

Measuring one particle reveals the other's state not because information traveled between them but because they carry copies of the same receipt. Entanglement doesn't violate causality because it's not communication. It's a shared record of a single past transaction. [I]

6. Kill Criteria

This paper is falsified if:

  1. A physical system is demonstrated where energy exchange is genuinely continuous (not quantized), contradicting the indivisibility claim.

  2. The identification E = hν ↔ φ·ν = 1 is shown to have no structural content beyond notational similarity (i.e., the structural consequences differ).

  3. Spectral line intensities are shown to have NO correlation with ΔB on S² as defined in Proposition 3.2.

  4. The electromagnetic field is shown to violate a necessary property of a decentralized ledger (e.g., a mechanism is found by which energy is not conserved in photon exchange).
