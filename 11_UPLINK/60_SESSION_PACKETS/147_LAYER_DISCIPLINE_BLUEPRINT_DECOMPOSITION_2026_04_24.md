## 0. Axiomatic Guard

This packet maps architecture across layers. The map is not the territory. The layers are a discipline for *where things belong*, not a hierarchy of worth. A feature at the substrate layer is not more important than a feature at the product layer — it just lives somewhere different and has different mutability, different blast radius, different K2 cost.

The risk we are guarding against: Grok's synthesis (CEO/CTO/Architect hats) pulled application-layer features down into the base layer because "clean minimalism." Clean minimalism *at the wrong layer* is calcification — the exoskeleton becomes uninhabitable.

## 2. Foundation Minimalism is Kernel Invariant VI

[S] From Paper 11 Doc 03 — `11_SKYZAI_CANON.md`:

> **Invariant VI: Foundation Minimalism.** The Foundation cannot compete with Tool DACs (Non-compete).

And from Paper 12 §VII — `12_SKYZAI_DIGITAL_CAPITAL_OF_THE_ENERGY_AGE.md`:

> *"No VM on L1. No governance theater. No permanent storage burden. No discretionary monetary policy. No sorcerer behind the curtain."*

And Paper 14 §V (The Covenant):

> *"The machine is small. The rules are hard. The organism is alive."*

These are not style preferences. They are load-bearing constraints. Every proposed feature must pass two tests:

**Test A — Does this feature belong in the substrate?** Substrate features are those whose absence breaks every higher layer. Money (ZAI/SKY). Consensus (DAG). Finality (Orange/Green). Routing (SPECTRE N:N). Fee structure. Receipts. Kernel Invariants. If a feature could live at the organ or product layer without the substrate caring, it does not belong in the substrate.

**Test B — Does this feature change with use cases?** Features that evolve per-use-case (UX flows, product bundles, deal structures, governance workflows, DAC-specific policies) belong above the substrate. The substrate should be *boring* and *unchanging*. Excitement lives in the organs and products built on top.

**Failure mode if these tests are not applied:** the substrate accretes features, becomes rigid by its own weight, cannot be modified without breaking users, and calcifies. The exoskeleton that was meant to host the intelligence explosion becomes a straitjacket.

## 5. The "exoskeleton for the intelligence explosion" test

From Paper 12 §VII. Every substrate feature must pass this:

> *"We are not building a casino for speculation. We are not building a governance experiment for the politically inclined. We are not building a database with a token attached. We are building the exoskeleton for the intelligence explosion."*

**Application:** if the feature is something the intelligence explosion needs *from the substrate* (money, consensus, finality, routing, settlement), it belongs here. If it's something the intelligence explosion would build *on top of* the substrate (governance workflows, coordination patterns, cluster rules, UX wrappers, product bundles), it does not.

The substrate is the dinosaur bone. Humans are not dinosaurs but we inhabit a world where dinosaurs walked. Their bones anchor our geology. Their extinction shaped our atmosphere. But we do not carry their flesh. The substrate's job is to be the bone — load-bearing, enduring, non-plastic — so that the intelligence explosion can hang its flesh on it without the bone creaking.
