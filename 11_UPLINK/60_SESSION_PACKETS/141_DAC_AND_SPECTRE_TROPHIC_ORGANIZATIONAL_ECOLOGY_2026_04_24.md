## 6. The Porter Value Chain Split — Load-Bearing Mechanism

Michael Porter's canonical value chain (1985):

**Primary activities:** Inbound logistics → Operations → Outbound logistics → Marketing & Sales → Service.

**Supporting activities:** Firm infrastructure, Human resource management, Technology development, Procurement.

Traditional corporations choose between (a) vertical integration (own both primary and supporting) or (b) outsourcing (externalize supporting to the market). Both strategies concentrate power and permit extraction at the seams.

**The Syntropic Dyadism split, new doctrine in this packet:**

| Porter's category | Under SD |
|---|---|
| **Primary activities** | **Per-DAC, competitively differentiated, competitive surplus permitted when η=0 remains intact** |
| **Supporting activities** | **Collectivized at SPECTRE, η=0 constitutional, commons not product** |

### 6.1 What this means operationally

**Primary (per-DAC, competitive):**
- Each DAC owns and differentiates its primary value chain
- Competition on operations, customer experience, pricing, feature development
- Schumpeterian creative destruction operates here cleanly
- Margin-efficiency pressure drives Layer 2 optimization
- Niche-creation pressure drives Layer 3 innovation

**Supporting (SPECTRE, collectivized commons):**
- **Firm infrastructure** → SPECTRE provides shared legal architecture, governance primitives, sovereign-identity protocols. No DAC builds its own regulatory compliance from scratch.
- **Human resource management** → SPECTRE provides shared labor standards, K4 portability between DACs, skill-recognition ontologies. No DAC locks in workers via non-competes or non-portable credentials.
- **Technology development** → SPECTRE maintains open-source reference implementations of shared primitives (authentication, receipts, consensus, observability). No DAC silos its tech-debt-burden on duplicated infrastructure.
- **Procurement** → SPECTRE provides shared commodity-market access, pooled-buying infrastructure, counterparty-reputation standards. No DAC negotiates from scratch what the ecosystem could standardize.

### 6.2 Why this inoculates against the classic capture pattern

The standard big-tech capture pattern (AWS, Google, Facebook) succeeds by:
1. Providing supporting-activity infrastructure at massive scale
2. Locking in via proprietary APIs and switching costs
3. Cross-subsidizing from captured-commons into primary-activity competition (AWS funds Alexa, Google Search funds Android, etc.)
4. Extracting rent on the commons indefinitely

Under the SD split:
1. Supporting activities are collectivized at SPECTRE from day one (no single DAC owns them)
2. η=0 at SPECTRE prevents rent-extraction on the commons
3. K4 guarantees DAC portability (no switching-cost lock-in)
4. Cross-subsidy is impossible: SPECTRE doesn't compete in primary activities; its only output is commons maintenance

**The structural claim:** this split is *the* mechanism by which an ecosystem can scale without producing a single captured commons. It is the organizational implementation of η=0 at ecosystem scale.

### 6.3 Boundary cases and edge risks

- **When supporting becomes primary**: a DAC may develop a genuinely novel primary-activity that happens to be useful as supporting-infrastructure for other DACs. Resolution: the DAC keeps the primary-activity competitively, SPECTRE may *adopt* an open-standard equivalent as commons. No forced contribution; voluntary and negotiated.
- **When primary becomes commons**: an established layer-2 practice may saturate into something every DAC needs. Resolution: SPECTRE may integrate it into the commons if DACs voluntarily converge on it as a standard. Usually via RFC-style processes.
- **When SPECTRE tries to compete**: forbidden. If SPECTRE ever attempts to provide primary-activity value as a for-revenue service, it has breached η=0 and must be corrected via A7. Sovereign-level event.
