# The Balance-Optimum Is Conditional

> **The one line.** "Output peaks at Φ=ν and *declines* with imbalance" (the downslope, `B = sin θ`) is a **theorem** — but only of systems that are **(i) conservation-bound** (finite budget: more ν is bought with less Φ), **(ii) complementary** (the task needs both *jointly*, they don't substitute), **(iii-a) friction-bearing in excess** (unmatched capacity becomes clutter/maintenance/paralysis, not inert surplus), and **(iii-b) symmetrically priced** — the cost of imbalance is the even ratio-metric `γ = (φ+ν)/2` (equivalently the payoff tracks `B = 2/(φ+ν)`), penalizing over-Φ and over-ν *one-for-one*. Drop (i), (ii), or (iii-a) and the downslope flattens to a *plateau* or inverts into a *specialization bonus*; **drop (iii-b) — keep an *asymmetric* cost — and an optimum survives but slides off the equator to a *tilted ratio* (a Cobb-Douglas peak at `a/b`, not `φ=ν`)**. The framework's error was never the balance-optimum; it was **universalizing** it *and* assuming its symmetry. `[correction folded in 2026-07-03 from the propagation-packet adversarial pass: (i)+(ii)+(iii-a) are necessary but not sufficient for the *symmetric* equator — a tilted-Cobb-Douglas or loose-Leontief system passes them yet peaks off-equator or only plateaus; (iii-b) is the seam.]`

## 1. The downslope is real — by reason, under conservation `[A]`

If Φ and ν trade off against a fixed budget (`Φ + ν = c`, the framework's own `φ·ν = 1` conservation is the multiplicative form of this), then by **AM-GM** the product is maximized at Φ=ν and falls monotonically as they diverge: pushing ν past balance *costs* Φ one-for-one, so the joint payoff declines. This is not "imbalance fails to help" — it is "imbalance actively hurts," because under a binding budget the excess of one factor **is** the deficit of the other. The downslope follows with the force of a theorem. `[A]`

## 2. The downslope is real — by reason, through friction of excess `[S/I]`

Even without a hard budget, unmatched capacity is rarely inert. Three named mechanisms make excess of one factor *actively degrade* the whole:

- **Excess ν over Φ (means over foresight):** Jensen's **free-cash-flow problem** — capital beyond good projects destroys value (empire-building, agency costs). The **resource curse** — an economy with vast resource capital but thin institutional/human capital does *worse* than a balanced one. The carpenter's thousandth chainsaw **clutters the finite workshop, demands maintenance, and taxes attention** — it does not sit weightless.
- **Excess Φ over ν (foresight over means):** **analysis-paralysis**, over-engineering, the "sterile abstraction" north pole — coherence that *sees* what it cannot *do* carries a real frustration/rumination cost, and can leave the over-planner worse off than a naive actor.

So the downslope has genuine qualitative support: past balance, the surplus factor turns from asset to burden. `[S]` the structure, `[I]` the mechanism-mapping.

## 3. But the same argument names its own limit `[S]`

Each argument above is **conditional**, and the conditions are load-bearing:

| Condition | If it holds | If it fails |
|---|---|---|
| **Conservation** (finite budget) | AM-GM downslope | free surplus → **plateau** (warehouse the extra chainsaws; lose nothing) |
| **Complementarity** (needs both jointly) | imbalance starves the scarce factor | **substitutability** → trade one for the other; ratio barely matters |
| **(iii-a) Costly excess** (friction) | surplus becomes burden | **inert/storable excess** → no downslope (plateau) |
| **(iii-b) Symmetric γ-price** | optimum sits *at* `φ=ν` — the equator | **asymmetric cost** → optimum is a *tilted ratio* (Cobb-Douglas `a/b`), not the equator; loose-Leontief → *plateau*, no decline |
| **Task rewards breadth** | balance wins | **task rewards depth** → **specialization *beats* balance** (the surgeon, the savant, comparative advantage) |

Where the right-hand column obtains, the equator is *not* the optimum — a corner or a *tilted* interior point is. Only **(iii-b)** actually delivers the framework's *specific* claim (the peak *at* `φ=ν`); (i)+(ii)+(iii-a) alone deliver at most "*an* optimum exists somewhere," which the asymmetric Munnell fit (`a=0.61 ≠ b=0.44`) shows is off-equator when it exists at all. This is exactly why aggregate production (markets reallocate excess; comparative advantage rewards specialization; factors substitute) shows a balance **trough**, not a peak ([VERDICT_BALANCE.md](../11_UPLINK/25_EXPERIMENTS/2026-07-02_production_function_form/VERDICT_BALANCE.md): d² > 0). No contradiction: aggregates violate conditions (i)–(iii).

## 4. The resolution — the balance-optimum is the law of the *finite mortal agent* `[S/I]`

The conditions under which the downslope holds — **finite budget, complementarity, costly excess** — are precisely the conditions of the framework's *own* home object: the **finite node `P_node = Φ × V`**, the **mortal substrate**, the **single D5 agent** with bounded time, attention, and means, doing a task that needs foresight *and* execution together. There, imbalance genuinely hurts, and the equator is genuinely optimal. The framework is **right where it lives.**

Its overreach is only the **universalization** — reading `B = sin θ`'s peak as a law of *everything*, including unconstrained, reallocating, substitutable aggregate systems where specialization wins. The honest form is scoped:

> **For a finite agent under a binding budget, performing a task whose factors are complementary and whose excess is costly, the payoff is maximized at balance and declines with imbalance. Relax any of those, and balance ceases to be optimal.** The equator is the optimum of the *constrained mortal*, not of the *cosmos*.

## 4b. Reconciliation with the Burrisphere — geometric vs empirical optimum `[A/S]`

The Burrisphere already carries most of this, and naming the split sharpens both. On the sphere, with `φ·ν = 1` **held as an identity**, the balance-optimum is not conditional at all — it is **`[A]` geometry**: `B = sin θ = 2/(φ+ν)`, and by AM-GM `φ+ν ≥ 2`, so `B ≤ 1` with the max at `φ=ν`. Equivalently the sphere's own **price `γ = 1/B = (φ+ν)/2`** rises monotonically with imbalance. The Burrisphere states the mechanism precisely: *"imbalance does not reduce the potential (`P∞ = 1` is conserved), it raises the price (`γ → ∞` toward the poles) and strands the potential unactualized."* That is the downslope, and on the sphere it is unconditional.

**The conditionality is entirely in the *transfer* to a finite real system** — the step from `P∞` (sphere identity) to `P_node = Φ × V` (an organism, task, economy). A real system reproduces the sphere's balance-optimum **iff it actually satisfies the sphere's premise**: that its two factors are **conservation-coupled** (a `γ`-like price is really paid for imbalance), **complementary**, and **costly in excess**. Those are the §3 conditions restated. Where they hold, the finite node *is* a point on the sphere and inherits the equator-optimum. Where they fail — unconstrained, substitutable, free-surplus systems — `P_node` is **not** a sphere point (its factors don't obey `φ·ν=1`), the `γ` price is not paid, and the balance-optimum simply does not transfer (aggregate production is the witness: it prices imbalance *negatively* — specialization pays).

So the honest split is:

| Claim | Tier | Status |
|---|---|---|
| On `S²` with `φ·ν=1`: `B` maxes at `φ=ν`; `γ` prices imbalance | `[A]` | unconditional (AM-GM) — **stands** |
| A given finite system's `P_node` sits on that sphere (pays the `γ` price) | `[S]` | **conditional** on conservation + complementarity + costly-excess + **symmetric `γ`-price** (else the optimum is tilted or a plateau, not the equator) |
| Therefore the equator is *that system's* optimum | `[S/I]` | inherited **only when** the transfer premise holds |

The Burrisphere's *"Dharma corollary"* ("unlimited means with falling foresight is lower usable power") is **exactly right for a system that pays the `γ` price** — a finite mortal agent under a budget — and **silently assumes the transfer** for systems that don't. The fix is one clause, not a retraction: mark the equator-optimum as the optimum of systems *on the sphere*, and state the test for whether a given system is on it.

## 5. What this changes

- **Concedes** (to the owner, by reason): the downslope is *real*, not a plateau, in the framework's proper domain. The seven-operator audit's "conjunction only" verdict was **too weak** — under conservation, conjunction *sharpens* into a genuine balance-optimum with a downslope. `[A]`
- **Bounds** (the membrane line): the optimum is **conditional**, not a universal geometric necessity. `B = sin θ`, the L4 apex, and "the equator is the optimum" must carry the scope clause of §4 or they overclaim — and aggregate data falsifies the unscoped version.
- **Reconciles**: the empirical anti-pattern and the dialectical downslope are the *same law seen in two regimes* — finite/conserved (peak) vs unconstrained/reallocating (trough). Both true; the framework was measuring the first, the Munnell test the second.

## 6. Kill criteria

1. Exhibit a **finite, budget-bound, complementary-task** agent for whom **imbalance outperforms balance** with no specialization/depth reward and no free surplus → the scoped downslope dies.
2. Exhibit an **unconstrained, substitutable** system where **balance strictly beats specialization** → the "specialization wins when conditions fail" clause dies.
3. Show that `φ·ν = 1` conservation does **not** license AM-GM (e.g. the factors are not positive reals) → §1 dies.
