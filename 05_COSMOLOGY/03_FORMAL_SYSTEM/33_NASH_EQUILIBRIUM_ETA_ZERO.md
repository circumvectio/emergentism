## Model A: no private return `[A]`

Let each player choose `e_i≥0`, and let every player's payoff be a strictly
decreasing function of total extraction `E=Σe_i`, with no private benefit from
their own `e_i`. Then `e_i=0` is strictly dominant for every player. This is
true by construction: extraction has only cost in the model.

The result is a useful control case, not evidence that real extraction is
irrational or that Justice is a Nash equilibrium.

## Model B: private return `[A]`

Let

```text
u_i(e_i,e_-i) = (1-δ) G(Σe_j) + δ g_i(e_i),   0<δ<1,
```

where `G` decreases with aggregate extraction and `g_i` increases locally with
private extraction. Whenever the marginal private gain at zero exceeds the
player's marginal share of collective loss, zero extraction is not a best
response. Positive-extraction equilibria may exist.

This counterexample kills the unconditional sentence “extraction is
irrational.” A short-horizon extractor, an agent with fresh hosts, or one who
externalizes costs can benefit.

## What can sustain low extraction `[S/C]`

Repeated interaction, monitoring, credible consequence, bounded exit,
reputation, aligned ownership, or other mechanism-design conditions can make a
low-extraction profile sustainable. The exact equilibrium depends on the
declared players, information, horizon, payoffs, enforcement, and outside
options. It must be proved for that game, not imported from this note.
